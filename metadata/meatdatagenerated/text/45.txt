Corner-based Timing Analysis (CTA) becomes more and more pessimistic as feature size shrinks. This trend has motivated the development of Statistical Static Timing Analysis (SSTA). In this paper, we propose a new path-based SSTA framework that allows the estimation of path delay distributions and delay correlations by propagating iteratively mean and variance of cell delay. These moments, conditioned on input slope and output load values, are pre-characterized by an improved method: log-logistic distribution based input signals and inverters as output load. In applications, the delay gains of this SSTA framework with respect to CTA are shown to be significant. It is also highlighted that the discrepancy of critical paths orderings obtained by SSTA and CTA depends on two factors: cell-tocell delay correlation and standard deviation of cell delay. & 2012 Elsevier Ltd. 1. Introduction

As process technology enters the nanometer era (o90 nm), Corner-based Timing Analysis (CTA) reaches its limits in constructing guaranteed bounds on circuit delays and becomes overly conservative. This pessimism comes from the assumption that all physical and environmental parameters are at their worst or best conditions simultaneously. In other words, extreme values are used to replace random process parameters and all correlations between them are set to either ‘‘1’’ or ‘‘1’’. More importantly, this pessimism increases as feature size shrinks. Statistical Static Timing Analysis (SSTA) offers a way out of this problem and has been much developed in recent years. SSTA is richer than CTA in nature, while offering more accurate estimations of actual circuit performance. Rather than simply determine corners and attempt to obtain a single value for delays, statistical timing engines propagate Probability Density Function (PDF) of timing variables. A typical approach uses the first [1–3] or second order [4–6] Taylor expansion of cell delays based on process variation models, like the grid [1] or the quad-tree model [2]. However, digital circuit designers have been reluctant in adopting this new generation of timing analysis due to some remaining weaknesses. A first weakness lies with the limited number of published delay models taking into account the effects of: Input/output (I/O) edges and output load values [7]. In fact, most approaches typically make a worst-case estimate of slope, which can lead to significant errors [7].

A second weakness is the lack of an effective technique to estimate correlations between cell delays and between path delays. For a first order cell delay model, estimation of correlations is feasible, as in [3]. However, first order approximations do not describe very well the complex relationships between cell delays and process parameters, and this renders estimated correlations inaccurate. For a second order delay model, estimation of delay correlations from process parameters seems complex [4] due to their large number and the empirical nature of some of them in actual statistical model cards [8]. In addition, both weaknesses are compounded in the existing techniques of correlation estimation and render them crude. A recent solution, in the context of process parameters, involves bounding techniques [9]. According to the algorithm to explore timing graphs, the existing parametric SSTA fall into one of the two categories: block-based [1–6] and path-based SSTA [7,9–13]. Nowadays, the block-based algorithm gains favor due to the linearity of its runtime as a function of circuit size and the ability of handling false paths [13]. However, these advantages may be limited by accuracy losses due to the intensive use of the MAX/MIN operation and to the lack of an accurate timing model to handle multiple-input collisions [14]. The path-based algorithm requires finding an appropriate set of candidate paths but avoids the MAX/MIN operation, while propagating PDF along paths. Thus, it avoids sources of error during simple path timing estimate and offers a simple and rigorous framework to validate the main proposal of this work: an accurate and fast method to calculate delay correlations between cells along paths. This algorithm could eventually be applied in the block-based algorithm, provided techniques to perform accurately MAX/MIN operations and to propagate efficiently general PDF become available. 4.2. Output load variations

We now compare the accuracy of the linear ramp and LL-based models in the following way:

a) collect 1000 simulated signals under various conditions for cell types: INV, BUF, NOR2, NAND2; b) for each signal, measure the 20–80% slope and approximate the signal respectively by linear ramp and LL-based model; c) normalized all measured and approximated signals by their own slope s, i.e. rescale the t axis of Fig. 5 to t/s; d) transform the point (t0/s, 0.5Vdd) of each signal to point (0, 0.5Vdd), i.e. take tn1⁄4(t–t0)/s, as shown in Fig. 6; e) define the grid of points t*1⁄4 1.5,1.4, y, 0, y, 1.4,1.5, and compute errors point by point for both linear ramp and LLbased approximations; f) compute the average errors of the following three signal subsamples: all linear ramp approximations, LL-based approximations with slopes, respectively, less than 100 ps and 200 ps, and all LL-based approximations.

The main drawback in modeling output load with capacitors having constant values is that they cannot capture output load variations, or more precisely, the impact of load variations on cell timings; in real circuit, the charges of input pins of all cells depend on process parameters and therefore are random variables. Moreover, the mean and variance of output load may depend on interconnects and the subsequent cells [23] because of an I/O coupling phenomenon that generates overshoots (or undershoots) [21], [22]. Modeling this dependency is necessary to observe accurately the values c of Cout. This requires further work unrelated to the current SSTA proposal and is not pursuit further here. To circumvent this and better represent what occurs in the next stage of a given cell in real circuits, our timing characterization uses inverters instead of capacitors to model output load. At the present time, most IC designers still use tools based on path-based CTA, and consider SSTA as a complement to CTA. However, results of SSTA do not necessarily coincide with those of CTA even when considering a single voltage Vdd and temperature T corner [25]. A well known reason explaining such differences, is the effect of path re-convergence that induces topological correlations between paths and creates false paths [13]. Block-based SSTA offers a solution to take this effect into account, but does not offer a solution to manage simultaneously process, temperature and voltage variations. This section shows there are at least two additional sources of discrepancy between orderings obtained with SSTA and CTA. They appear even on an isolated path. One of these sources could explain the rare unexpected optimism of CTA pointed out in [25]. A typical Computer-Aided-Design (CAD) tool based on CTA, like RTL Compiler [16], extracts a set of N critical paths for optimization of circuit design. These N critical paths are ordered by decreasing worst delay according to CTA, i.e. the first critical path has the maximal worst delay; the second one has the second maximal value, etc. However, if path delays of the same set are computed by SSTA, the ordering of these paths may be different with that of CTA. 7. Conclusions

Comparing (37) with (38), the quantities rkm are fixed to the constant ‘‘1’’, meaning that all cell delays are perfectly correlated. In (38a), sgdkUyk=3 are the pessimistic standard deviation values considered by CTA, and thus are the duals of sgdk in (37). Therefore, the discrepancy of orderings comes from two factors: cell-to-cell delay correlation rkm and standard deviation of cell delay sgdk. On the one hand, in the context of timing analysis, rkm varies in the interval [0,1], and it is rare that rkm1⁄41 if kam. However, rkm is systematically set to ‘‘1’’ in (38), which introduces a first difference between wpd and spd. On the other hand, according to the CTA, wgdk is computed by setting each process parameter pl to a corner value. Therefore it is unlikely that yk is exactly equal to 3 in (38a), which introduces a second source of difference in orderings. In this paper, we have developed a path-based SSTA framework that allows a fast estimation of path delay distributions and delay correlations by propagating iteratively mean and variance of cell delay. Its accuracy derives mainly from a realistic estimation of delay correlations and the use of log-logistic and active load (inverters) models for input slope and output load. As a result, the proposed engine gives estimates of path delay means and standard deviations with relative errors less than 5% and 10%, respectively.
