Keywords: Statistical timing analysis Statistical operation Deep neural network Deep learning Critical path Delay

Discrete statistical static timing analysis (SSTA) performs the timing analysis by using statistical maximum and convolution operations. The maximum is basically a non-linear operator and it is not a simple task to capture the skewness introduced by it. On the other hand, the convolution has a potential to “blow-up” the number of discrete samples as we going deep inside the timing graph and hence, results in exponential timing complexity. Therefore, in this paper we present novel deep neural network based operations which can accurately approximate the signal arrival-time’s distributions with linear-time complexity. The various deep neural network (DNN) architectures have been used to implement both the maximum and the convolution operations using proper training dataset. Simulation results on various benchmark circuits (ISCAS 85, ISCAS 89, and ITC 99) show that the proposed method estimate the mean and standard deviation (STD) of critical path delay distribution with an average error of 0.75% and 2.56% as compared to Monte Carlo (MC), respectively. Our SSTA speeds up the traditional discrete approach by a factor of 20.7x on average. Furthermore, the PDF obtained from our method matches the ones obtained from MC with a reasonable error. Furthermore, we have proposed multi-wise maximum operations to reduce the arrival-time computational complexity at multi-inputs gates. Comparing to MC, the proposed method shows 0.97% and 2.58% average error in mean and STD respectively and the speeding up factor reaches 24.4x on average for all benchmarks.

1. Introduction

Timing analysis is of a great interest in digital integrated circuits (IC) design and optimization. Wherein the timing tool ensures to meet the timing constraints of the chips. Static timing analysis (STA) has been presented as a versatile method for fast and accurate yield approximation ( Sapatnekar, 2004 ). Although STA has been a mature timing tool for many years, with entering the nanometer regime, the process parameter variations become considerable and choosing the worst-case values for parameters results in very pessimistic delay estimation. To cope with this pessimism, statistical static timing analysis (SSTA) was proposed to take the variations of the parameters into account ( Berkelaar, 1997 ). In SSTA most of the timing parameters are modeled as Random Variables (RVs) with an expected value and a certain amount of deviation from their nominal values. Block-based SSTA is known to be the most popular and efficient method for statistical timing analysis, which involves a PERT-like

traversal of the circuit graph ( Abbaspour, Fatemi & Pedram, 2006 ; Chang & Sapatnekar, 2005 ; Devgan & Kashyap, 2003 ; Kang, Paul & Roy, 2005 ; Visweswariah et al., 2006 ; Zhang, Chen, Hu & Chen, 2006 ). As opposed to this, the path-based SSTA tries to find the most critical path among a set of selected paths ( Amin et al., 2005 ; Mangassarian & Anis, 2005 ). However, the path-based method has exponential runtime in circuits with many critical paths or balanced circuits ( Sapatnekar, 2004 ). In the block-based method, the statistical Addition and Maximum operations are employed to calculate the arrival times (ATs) at every node of the graph. The addition operation is trivial, however the Maximum due to its nonlinear behavior requires much more effort s ( Blaauw, Chopra, Srivastava & Scheffer, 2008 ). Considerable number of SSTA methods in literature use parametric models to approximate the above operations. Initial works on parametric SSTA used first-order canonical form to represent delays and ATs ( Blaauw et al., 2008 ). In the work of Berkelaar (1997) , variations of process parameters modeled as independent Gaussian random variables then delays and ATs are expressed as linear function of Gaussian distributions. The Maximum of two random variables is a nonlinear function and introduces skewness in most cases (i.e. maximization of two Gaussians results in a non-Gaussian random variable) ( Chopra, Zhai, Blaauw & Sylvester, 2006 ). Moments are approximated based on the concept of tightness probability proposed by Clark (1961) . Neglecting the skewness due to the Maximum in the first-order canonical model results in serious inaccuracies in estimating timing quantities ( Chopra et al., 2006 ). On the other hand, in emerging VLSI technologies, not all the process variations have a Gaussian distribution. In addition, delays are a complicated non-linear function of parameters variations. Therefore, quadratic canonical models have been deployed to account for both non-Gaussian variations and nonlinearities ( Bhardwaj, Ghanta & Vrudhula, 2006 ; Cheng et al., 2012 ; Chopra et al., 2006 ; Ramprasath, Vijaykumar & Vasudevan, 2016 ). Cheng et al. (2012) used quadratic delay model and develop a methodology to approximate the moments of maximum based on Fourier series. The proposed maximum is capable of handling non-Gaussian variations as well as non-linear dependency. Although accuracy of timing models increases, it is at the cost of additional computational complexity. In order to reduce the complexity of quadratics model Ramprasath et al. (2016) presented skew-normal delay model to handle the skewness of the maximum along with non-Gaussian parameters. At calculation step of arrival times, this method matches the mean, standard deviation and the third moment (skewness) of both statistical operations. Skew-normal models are marginally expensive as compared to linear models, however, like quadratic models, they suffer from handling the difficulties rises from the maximum when parameter’s spatial correlations are taken into the account and, thus, expressing it in canonical form. A Gaussian-mixture model has been proposed in Tsukiyama and Fukui (2011) to approximate the maximum operator distribution and to reduce the error of the Gaussian assumption. A conditional max operation has been proposed by Chung, Xiong, Zolotov and Abraham (2012) to compute the path criticalities, however the approximation error remains considerable. Optimization of digital circuits requires repetitive updates on circuits’ structure (e.g. gate re-sizing, wire re-routing, buffer insertion) hence, incremental SSTA has been proposed ( Visweswariah et al., 2006 ) and ( Huang & Wong, 2015 ; Lee, Jiang, Li, Chiu & Yang, 2015 ; Sinha, Visweswariah, Venkateswaran, Xiong & Zolotov, 2012 ) to allow the designer perform the timing analysis only on the updated portion of the circuit rather than re-simulating the whole circuit. Ignoring the changes due to circuit updates, leads to considerable error if the changes affect a large portion of the circuit ( Huang & Wong, 2015 ). However, with local updating, a very small portion of updated area (e.g. only arrival time of an input of the preceding gate) may change. Therefore, for the sake of the efficiency it is necessary to regard the changes of timing information of that small portion only ( Sinha et al., 2012 ). Sinha et al. (2012) presented a reversible-max operation to avoid redundant pair-wise maximums. The main idea of reversible-max is if we have a random variable Z = max( X 1 , X 2 ,..., X K −1 , X K , ..., X N ) and X k changes to X ∗ k after incremental updating then the updated max will be Z = max ( Z ∗, X ∗ k ) where Z ∗= max( X 1 , X 2 ,..., X K −1 , ..., X N ) which lessens the timing overhead considerably. Fig. 1 shows the above concept. Another SSTA alternative to handle the non-Gaussian distributions is to model gates and interconnections delays and arrival times all as a set of discrete samples representing their PDFs or CDFs ( Agarwal, Blaauw, Zolotov & Vrudhula, 2003 ; Devgan & Kashyap, 2003 ; Liou, Cheng, Kundu & Krstic, 20 01 , 20 02 ; Naidu, 2002 ). The discrete events are obtained by sampling the continuous random variables at specified sampling step. Generally, the discrete SSTA procedure contains two operations; the convolution (sum) of the samples for adding up the samples sets and

the statistical discrete maximum to find the latest arrival time of nodes. In Devgan and Kashyap (2003) , a fast algorithm has been proposed in which gates’ delays are modeled as piece-wise constant PDFs and arrival times as piece-wise linear CDFs using a small number of sample points (maximum of 7 points). However, the arrival times at graph nodes are approximated up to quadratic. Liou et al. (2001) have proposed an accurate technique to determine the probabilistic events of circuit’s delay distribution. The proposed technique employs discrete Addition and Maximum to compute the arrival-times at graph nodes. Naidu (2002) models the delays by a sequence of impulses (train impulse) implying their PDFs to perform SSTA. Although, both previous approaches runtimes grow exponentially with respect to the circuit size, they can accurately predict the delay distribution. Most of the natural phenomena, exhibit a non-linear behavior (e.g. solutions, weather, chaos, etc.) and it is rather hard accurately model these inherent non-linearities in the complex systems. Among all the existing non-linear mathematical models, Deep Neural Networks (DNN) models have been evolved for the past few years and shown to be the most reliable and efficient tool to model or predict many tasks in various fields of research. A considerable amount of works can be found in the literature which employed DNNs. Cincotti, Gallo, Ponta and Raberto (2014) model IPEX’s (Italian Power Exchange) electrical spot-prices by using computational intelligence (CI) approach. The proposed approach was based on a discrete framework of econometric time series, hence, the authors have been implemented a time-delayed DNN to model the time-series. DNN models are also applicable in forecasting prices and trends as Weron (2014) has been reviewed some of the most notable research in electrical price forecasting. In another study, Kamar, Ahmad, Kamsah and Mustafa (2013) used a high accuracy DNN-based model to predict compressor input power, cooling load and the performance coefficients of an air conditioning system. Srinivasan and Saghir (2013) showed that a well-trained DNN model can accurately quantify the thermodynamic attributes of binary metal alloys. Pedro, Nyandoro and John (2009) designed an DNN-based anti-lock braking system and the results show that the DNN model provides a better output in comparison to PIDbased systems. In other research, Mohagheghi, Harley, Habetler and Divan (2009) proposed a monitoring technique based on DNN to determine the effect of parameter variations in electronic power circuits. The field programmable gate array (FPGA) implementation of DNN has been presented by Jahanirad, Farokh and Mohammadi, (2010) for an adaptive frequency domain equalizer in digital mobile communications. Along with the aforementioned applications, the DNNs has been an active research topic in the field of IC design as well ( Avci M, 2008 ; Lin et al., 2020 ; Chen, Wang and Duan, 2019 ; Su, Chen, Cheng & Chiu, 2018 ; Xu et al., 2018 ). The DNN-based approach allows us to approximate the distributions accurately with linear time complexity. The key contributions of this paper are the followings:

1. We have proposed a smart DNN-based model with linear-time complexity to implement the SSTA operations which can overcome the non-linearity of the operations and estimate the exact output with reasonable error. 2. We have modeled the convolution operation with a Deep Neural Network model which requires deep learning concepts to train the NN model to acquire the best performance. 3. In order to lessens the computational time and prevent multiple pair-wise maximum operations at multi-fanin nodes, a new DNN-based maximum operations have been proposed to calculate the latest arrival time at multi-fanin nodes with only a single maximum operation.

Summation of two discrete RVs is similar to propagating the discrete events using two independent operations: Shift with scaling and Grouping . The shift with scaling operation shifts each cell’s delay event by each individual sample of the input events and scales the cell delay with multiplying the probability of both corresponding events. After Grouping the samples, the final distribution of the arrival-time at the output of the OR gate will have 7 samples ( Fig. 3 ). 3.2. Max operation using DNN

Modeling the max operation is a crucial task in block-based statistical timing analysis. Most of the recent methods strive to present a closed-form formula for approximating the Maximum in parametric SSTA which are either computational expensive ( Singh & Sapatnekar, 2007 ) or inaccurate ( Berkelaar, 1997 ). Statistical discrete maximization has been used in discrete SSTA to accurately propagate the timing distributions, but with more timing complexity (i.e. Quadratic) Devgan and Kashyap (2003) . In this section, an accurate and time-efficient DNN-based method is developed in order to compute the maximum. In our SSTA method, we deal with discrete distributions. Therefore, for obtaining the discrete probability distribution for the maximum, we will confront with three independent cases for each sample k of the output distribution. Suppose random variable Z = maximum(X, Y), hence, we have:

Case 1: when X = k and Y < k the probability of Z = k will be Pr ( X = k ) Pr ( Y < k ) . Case 2: X < k and Y = k . Similarly, this happens with probability Pr ( X < k ) Pr ( Y = k ) . Case 3: X = k and Y = k . And the probability of this case happening is Pr( X = k )Pr( Y = k ).

There are various types of distributions which will be experienced by NN-MAX when SSTA traverses throughout the circuit. The dataset for NN-MAX has been prepared according to Fig. 8 . First, an adequate number (e.g. 50 0 0) of Gaussian distribution is created with a mean and standard deviation in the range of [0, 20] and each distribution is expressed by exactly 100 samples in the fixed interval of [ −3 σ . 3 σ ]. The generated data are registered in “Type 0 ” reservoir, which means the corresponding input/output vectors (100 samples) for each randomly generated normal distribution are saved in an appropriate data structure Fig. 9 . Next steps are responsible for generating non-Gaussian distributions, which were called type 1 , type 2 , ..., type n . To generate the N i data for type i reservoir, two sets of size N i are selected randomly from type i-1 ′ s reservoir, then the maximum of the distribution pairs (the distributions with same index from two sets) are calculated using (14) , and the corresponding results are registered in type i reservoir. According to our simulations, 10 types of reservoir is essential to generate different non-Gaussian distributions and more than 10 Max operations will generate similar distribution. The training dataset is constructed by randomly selection of the data from reservoirs ( Fig. 10 ). One needs to cover the entire range of data (all types of distributions) in order to strengthen the ability of the DNN to identify and estimate different types of distributions. Appendix: Extended Maximum

In this paper we have proposed a Deep Neural Network model to approximate both statistical SUM and MAX operations. Our main motivation for this was that the MAX operation is inherently a non-linear function which creates a non-Gaussian distribution as the output. Therefore, we chose DNN model as it is well known to handle and approximate non-linear functions. We have shown that using DNN models lessens the number multiplications and hence reduce computational time. Accordingly, we have modeled the convolution with DNN in order to have linear operator. Moreover, based on the same reasoning we have proposed triple-wise and quad-wise maximum operators to achieve the minimum runtime. With experimentation, we have verified that the fitting accuracy of the proposed SSTA as compared to other SSTA methods and results show that our method has a reasonable pessimism at predicting the mean of arrival times. However, as a comparison to parametric SSTA the NN-SSTA results in more accurate distribution. Due to DNNs input size limitation, the proposed method only functions with trained input size otherwise one should adjust the input sample size (either up or down sampling) to predefined size.
