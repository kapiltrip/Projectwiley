A R T I C L E I N F O

A B S T R A C T

Keywords: Static timing analysis Complex network Machine learning Layout Routing

Due to the high cost of timing signoff in the routing stage of VLSI (very large-scale integration), timing analysis in the placement stage is usually used to assist the implementation. Still, uncertainty exists due to the lack of detailed wiring information. Considering the close relationship between signal propagation and circuit topology, we propose a machine-learning method to predict the circuit timing using complex network features and construct basic and augmented models for comparison. Firstly, in the placement stage, the fundamental circuit features related to timing optimization are extracted, and the complex network features on the timing path are computed by complex network modeling, based on which the dataset for machine learning is generated. Secondly, we use machine learning algorithms to compare the performance of the two models in predicting circuit timing. Experimental results show that the augmented model combining complex network features outperforms the basic model in timing prediction accuracy. The augmented model achieves a 12.5 % reduction in MSE (mean squared error) for predicting slack, reaching 0.004515. It also shows a 1.06 % improvement in R 2 , reaching 0.9314, and a 0.54 % improvement in r, reaching 0.9652. For predicting delay, the MSE is reduced by 11.69 %, reaching 0.000302, while R 2 improves by 0.03 % to 0.9973, and r improves by 0.02 % to 0.9987. The deviation in predicting WNS (worst negative slack) is reduced by 6.53 %, reaching 5.30 %, and for TNS (total negative slack), it is reduced by 1.59 %, reaching 12.38 %. The AUC (area under the ROC (receiver operating characteristic) curve) value for identifying critical paths increases by 0.26 %, reaching 0.9873. These results demonstrate the efficiency of the proposed method in utilizing complex network features for predicting circuit timing.

1. Introduction

Designers employ timing analysis to evaluate circuit timing performance, ensuring that the circuit’s timing behavior meets design requirements. The accuracy and efficiency of this process significantly impact chip quality and cost. If post-routing timing analysis reveals violations, designers must modify and optimize circuits until timing convergence is achieved. To conserve substantial design resources during the design process, timing analysis in the layout phase becomes critical.

Timing convergence in very large-scale integration (VLSI) design is one of the key challenges in achieving the expected operating frequencies and delay constraints in circuit requirements [1,2]. Static timing analysis (STA) is one of the most common techniques used in digital design verification to resolve various timing issues and accelerate timing convergence in chip design tasks [3].

3.1. Framework of the timing prediction method

Fig. 1 shows the framework of the proposed method for predicting circuit timing using complex network features. It consists of two main stages: dataset construction at the top and model construction at the bottom of the figure.

In this work, we do not use cell delay and transition time correlated with transition rate as the features for the prediction. There are two main styles of timing analysis, i.e., graph-based timing analysis (GBA) mode and path-based timing analysis (PBA) mode. GBA mode can quickly obtain timing analysis results. However, the results are often pessimistic because it uses the worst signal transition time across all input pins of the cell to calculate cell delay. PBA uses the specific transition time of each pin to compute the cell delay and propagates the transition time along the path to obtain a relatively accurate delay for each multi-input stage. However, numerous data paths and complex calculations lead to a massive time overhead [17].

A lack of information on cell delay and transition rates could affect the proposal’s performance. Instead, we use the calculated maximum, minimum, and average values of the features of the complex network corresponding to the cells along the data path. We expect these parameters to provide comprehensive information and insights for timing prediction as an alternative.

The first stage is dataset construction, where the timing features and timing labels are extracted during the placement and routing phases, respectively. The circuits in the benchmark are placed to obtain the placement, which is transformed into a complex network representation through complex network modeling, and complex network features are computed. 3.2. Complex network modeling

Subsequently, the circuit placement is routed using the Vivado [20] routing tool. Static timing analysis is performed on the circuit to generate path-based timing reports. A timing path refers to the propagation path of a signal from a clock-triggered flip-flop to a target register in digital circuits consisting of a series of logic gates and wires. The work extracts timing path slacks and delays from post-routing timing reports, which serve as labels for the dataset. In addition, labels indicating critical paths are generated based on positive and negative slacks. A path with negative slack is considered a critical path.

Finally, the obtained features and labels are matched according to each timing path, generating a data record for each path. The manipulation is repeated for all the circuits to generate the final dataset.

3.4. Machine learning methods

At the placement stage, we use complex network modeling to extract complex network features to construct the dataset. We extract module locations and module-to-module connection information from the placement file. This data forms the basis for creating a two-dimensional undirected weighted complex network. In the network representation, the circuit modules are represented as nodes, the connections are represented as edges, and the half-perimeter distance between connected modules is the weight assigned to the edge.

4.3. TNS and WNS prediction

4.4. Critical path detection

Worst negative slack (WNS) and total negative slack (TNS) are two critical metrics used in circuit timing analysis to evaluate the quality of timing constraints. WNS represents the least amount of negative slack among all timing paths. When slack is less than zero, it indicates a timing violation. Therefore, WNS represents the most severe instance of timing violations in the entire circuit. TNS, on the other hand, is the sum of the negative slack values across all timing paths, providing an assessment of the overall quality of the timing constraints.

5. Discussion

To address the uncertainty issue in traditional methods for timing analysis during the placement phase of circuit design, this paper proposes a machine-learning approach that utilizes complex network features to predict post-routing circuit timing. Two models are constructed, including a basic model and an augmented model. Experimental results demonstrate that the augmented model, incorporating complex network features, outperforms the basic model in timing prediction. It highlights the efficiency of complex network features in enhancing the performance of timing prediction models. This approach offers a novel perspective and method for timing optimization in the VLSI design field.

The transition rate determines cell delay by altering transition time, which affects path delay due to uncertainty in arrival time. Therefore, cell delay and transition rate are mutually dependent factors in timing analysis.
