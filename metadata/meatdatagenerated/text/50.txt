I. INTRODUCTION

Classical static timing analysis, though simple and efficient, has several severe drawbacks.

1) It can be overly pessimistic due to a simplified model. 2) It can also be overly optimistic: a chip might fail although static timing analysis detects no problem. 3) It works only for acyclic timing graphs. Only heuristics are known for timing graphs with directed cycles. 4) Arrival times and slacks are not continuous functions of the input. Changing an asserted arrival time slightly at some input may change a computed arrival time at some output drastically. 5) Slacks are often too optimistic. A positive slack of x ps does not mean (as one would expect) that an additional delay of x ps at this place could be tolerated without creating timing violations.

Slack in Static Timing Analysis

Jens Vygen

Abstract—The notion of slack is central in static timing analysis and very large scale integration (VLSI) design in general. Negative slack means that a timing constraint is violated, while a positive slack of x ps is intended to mean that an extra delay of x ps (or a smaller delay by x ps in early mode) could be tolerated. However, this property does not hold with the standard static timing analysis model. The paper defines slack properly, shows how to compute it efficiently, and proves that it has the intended properties. The proposed idea is based on enhanced slew propagation.

Index Terms—Delay budgeting, physical design, slack, slack distribution, static timing analysis, VLSI design.

We argue that the last four mentioned drawbacks will become more and more important as extensive minimization of power consumption is done because this leads to large parts of the chip with very small slack. The first point also becomes more important and has to be addressed by statistical methods and/or transistor-level timing; however, it is not yet clear how to make this effective for large designs. In this paper, we will show how to extend the theory of static timing analysis using enhanced slew propagation to resolve the other four problems. We propose a safe model that is as pessimistic as necessary, but not more. It probably works for general timing graphs, is continuous, and computes reliable slacks. This is a key basis for timing optimization. Moreover, computing the information is almost as simple and fast as classical propagation. In the final part of the paper, we briefly review slack distribution (with many applications in physical design) and show how our enhanced timing model extends to optimum slack distributions in a natural and well-defined sense studied before. This is a theoretical paper. The impact in practice will have to be determined by future work.

II. STATIC TIMING ANALYSIS

We briefly describe the classical static gate-level timing analysis framework essentially introduced by Hitchcock et al. [9], Agrawal [1], Jouppi [10], and Ousterhout [15]. We point out several weaknesses and generalize the model by enhanced slew propagation in order to remove these drawbacks. Based on the generalized model, a new theory of static timing analysis is developed.

If all bits at registers and primary inputs remain constant from one clock cycle to the next, then the voltage remains constant everywhere on the chip. But if at least one bit changes, this information must be propagated through the network. The information that we propagate is called a signal. A signal consists of: 1) the information whether the voltage is rising (the bit changes from 0 to 1) or falling (the bit changes from 1 to 0); 2) the time when the voltage change occurs with respect to clock cycle 0; 3) a measure of how fast the voltage changes; and 4) information on the origin of the signal (a primary input or register). The reason for 1) is the significantly different propagation delays. This partially makes up for ignoring the logical functions. As both rising and falling voltages can occur, we usually have pairs of signals. In fact, we normally have four signals at each point as we have to consider the earliest and latest possible voltage changes in each direction (rising or falling). 2) and 3) are the standard information in static timing analysis: the characteristics of a voltage change over time are encoded by two numbers: the arrival time (usually the 50% transition time) and the slew (usually the difference of 10% and 90% transition times). In the following, we will not restrict ourselves to this specific definition of arrival time and slew. However, we require that delays depend monotonely on slew. This is always the case if the point in time considered as arrival time is early enough in the voltage transition. All arrival times of signals refer to a specific clock cycle. Since we want to check the timing conditions for the complete chip and for all cycles simultaneously, we need information 4) on the origin of the signal, in particular its local frequency. Since the propagation delay of a signal depends on its slew (more precisely, on the voltage function over time), we have to propagate forward. So for each primary input x and each output pin x of a register, we consider a set S(x) := {(x, early, rise), (x, early, fall), (x, late, rise), (x, late, fall)} of four possible signals. With each signal σ ∈S(x) in point x, we associate its arrival time at(x, σ) ∈R and some information slew(x, σ) ∈Λ on the shape of the voltage function. We assume that Λ (the slew range) is a finite closed interval of R. Smaller values correspond to “stronger” signals, where the voltage goes faster from 0 to 1 (or vice versa). Propagation is carried out in the so-called timing graph G, a directed graph whose vertex set V (G) contains all pins. As usual, the elements of the arc set E(G) represent connections within the gates as well as wire connections. Each arc e = (p, q) ∈E(G) (also called a propagation segment) is associated with two monotonely increasing Lipschitz continuous functions θe : Λ →R and λe : Λ →Λ. (A function f : Λ →R is called Lipschitz continuous if there is an α ≥0, the Lipschitz constant, such that |f(x) −f(y)| ≤α|x −y| for all x, y ∈Λ.) θe and λe describe the delay and slew at the endpoint of the propagation segment as a function of the input slew. In most cases, but not necessarily always, the value of θe will be positive; it is called the delay of the signal along e. To allow different delays for different transitions, we use parallel arcs and introduce a mapping σe : S →S ∪{∅} for each arc e, where S := Ω× {early, late} × {rise, fall} and Ωis the set of origin labels. Usually, σe(r, η, ζ) = ∅for all r ∈Ωand all (η, ζ) ̸= (η′, ζ′), for some η′ ∈{early, late}, ζ′ ∈{rise, fall} (meaning that the arc is used only for (η′, ζ′) signals), and σe(r, η′, ζ′) = (r, η′, ζ′′) for some ζ′′ ∈ {rise, fall} and all r ∈Ω. For each net with source p and each sink q of this net, we then have four parallel arcs (p, q), one for each element of {early, late} × {rise, fall}. Moreover, for each gate, we have an internal timing subgraph defined in the timing rule. For a start, we omit internal arcs of registers; then, the timing graph G is acyclic. The timing graph is then a general directed graph, not necessarily acyclic. We still have arrival times and slews for signals S(x) for all vertices x with zero in-degree and required arrival times (and slews) at vertices of zero out-degree. For other vertices, we may or may not have required arrival times. Other timing constraints can be modeled using so-called tests: a test is an ordered pair e = ((x, σ), (y, σ′)) that represents the constraint that at(x, σ) ≤at(y, σ′). For example, each register has at least two tests, a setup test and a hold test. After having computed all arrival times, the inequalities defined by tests can be checked easily. In the following, we ignore adjusts for the sake of a simpler notation. Note, however, that they can be included easily without any complication. General timing graphs with test arcs allow us to encode that an arbitrary signal must arrive before another one. Thus, one can include clock signals in timing analysis without having to fix their arrival times a priori. Moreover, circuits with complex internal structures, dynamic logic, and asynchronous signals can be handled by introducing appropriate tests.

Classical static timing analysis is simple and efficient but has several major drawbacks: it may conclude that a chip works although it does not, results are not continuous functions of the input (in contrast to reality), convergence in nonacyclic timing graphs cannot be proved, and slacks are often too optimistic. We proposed a modification, based on enhanced slew propagation, that is just as efficient but has none of these drawbacks. In particular, we redefined the notion of slack, which is central in very large scale integration (VLSI) design, properly. We also addressed how the popular method of distributing slack is affected. A thorough experimental study with an industrial timing engine and a combination with statistical timing is left for future work.
