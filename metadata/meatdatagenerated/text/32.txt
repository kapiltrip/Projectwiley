Statistical gate-delay modeling with copulas

Walter Schneider

HAW Landshut, University of Applied Sciences, Landshut, Germany

A R T I C L E I N F O

A B S T R A C T

Keywords: Statistical gate-delay Process variations Copulas

The growing impact of process variations on circuit performance has become a major concern for deep-submicron integrated circuit design, resulting in numerous SSTA-algorithms. The acceptance of such algorithms in industry however will be dependent on modeling the real silicon behavior in SSTA. This includes that the statistical gate-delay models must consider arbitrary process variations and dependencies. In this paper, we introduce the innovative concept of Copulas to handle this topic. A complete Matlab based framework starting from process parameter statistics up to the computation of the statistical gate-delay distribution is presented. Experimental results demonstrate the importance of accounting realistic process variations.

1. Introduction

1. Ignoring variations of slope and load 2. Reduction of the amount of varying process parameters, mostly assumed to be normally distributed 3. Limitation of the parameters’ dependencies by means of a simple linear correlation coefficient.

In CMOS digital design, accurate timing characterization of logic gates and digital circuits is essential. For Static Timing Analysis (STA) it is common practice that two dimensional lookup-tables are used for describing the delay of cells dependent on input slope and load capacitance. By technology downscaling deep into the nanometer era, process variations increase significantly and will cause high variability in circuit performance. The photolithography process is one important source of process variations which impacts the channel length L and the channel width W of a MOS transistor. Another well known example is the random dopant fluctuation (RDF) which affects the transistor threshold voltage. The traditional way to treat variations is to consider process corners at which a logic cell has its worst-case delay. However, for an increasing number of process variations this corner-based approach using STA becomes computationally expensive. Furthermore, with a decrease in the transistor size the σ

μ-ratio of the varying parameters proportionally increase and leads to overly pessimistic and suboptimal designs. Statistical Static Timing Analysis (SSTA) has been proposed as an improvement since the actual statistics of the process parameters are considered via random variables (RV). In general, the SSTA algorithms can be classified into two categories: the block-based and the pathbased algorithms. Although many research papers addressing both techniques have been published in the past it must be asserted, that SSTA is still not widely utilized in industry. One main reason is due to some simplified assumptions for statistical gate-delay modeling, such as:

Despite of all enhancements concerning these issues (e.g. addressing non-normal distributions in [1–3]) a common view on the complete process parameter statistics given from a semiconductor manufacturer and its translation on a statistical gate-delay model is still missing. The most promising approach in this context is the research paper [4], where the authors have introduced the Generalized Lambda Distribution (GLD) together with rank correlations. Unfortunately, the presented results do not show the actual process statistics and their correlations. Furthermore, Monte Carlo runs are necessary to get rank correlation coefficients as well as the GLD parameters. Additionally, the high computational cost of SSTA algorithms turned out as a stumbling block for practical usage leading to several on-chip variation (OCV) approaches in recent years. In parametric on-chip variation (POCV) each cell is characterized by a delay variation σobtained through Monte-Carlo SPICE simulation [5]. Using the Liberty Variation Format (LVF) which reports cell-delay variation for different slope/load combinations improves the accuracy for 16 nm designs and below [6,7]. Cadence Liberate Trio Characterization Suite generates LVF libraries using statistical SPICE models [8] - however problem (3) from above has not exhaustively been solved. 2. Process variations

2.1.3. Oxide thickness variation (OTV) OTV is caused by atomic scale roughness between silicon and gate dielectric [13]. In 45 nm technologies and beyond, OTV is becoming more dominant as Tox approaches to less than 2 nm. OTV introduces variations of the gate current Ig which leads to variations in Vth. Also the electron mobility μis influenced by OTV. Among the discussed sources of process variability and others (e.g. Optical Proximity Effect (OPE) or carrier velocity saturation with impact on channel length and electron mobility) LER and RDF have attracted most attention because it is expected that variations in L and Vth have the most impact on performance variation.

Amongst a huge number of parameters, which affect the performance behavior of MOSFET devices, the geometry parameters channel length (L), channel width (W) as well as the physical parameters oxide thickness (Tox), electron mobility (μ) and threshold voltage (Vth) are the most important [9]. We briefly outline the major sources of variation of these parameters.

Varying process parameters are usually treated as RVs. The assumption about normally distributed parameters is very convenient for SSTA. However this does not always reflect the true behavior. 4. Dependency modeling using copulas

Copulas are helpful in the understanding of dependency at a deeper level meaning that the dependency is not restricted to the simple case of linear correlation. Copulas combine arbitrary marginal distribution functions (df) to a joint distribution function with a variety of possible dependency models, which are isolated from the marginals. From these dependency models rank correlations as in [4] or coefficients of tail dependency can be derived. In the following the most important basic properties of copulas are summarized. The reader is referred to [26] for further detailled information.

Definition 4.1. A d-dimensional copula C is a df on the unit hypercube [0, 1]d with standard uniform marginal distributions, C ∶[0, 1]d → [0, 1].

Using familiar rules from mathematical probability theory in case of an analytical or polynomial parametric model: e.g. sum/product of RVs or the multivariate density transformation theorem [22,23]. 2. Conversely, if C is a copula and F1, ... , Fd are univariate dfs, then the function F in (7) is a joint df with the margins F1, ... , Fd.

6. Experimental results

Our approach for statistical gate-delay modeling has been implemented in Matlab and tested using an inverter in 45 nm CMOS technology. First, a sensitivity SPICE analysis confirms that the discussed parameters in Section 2: L, W, Vth, Tox and VMAX (for modeling the decrease in mobility μdue to velocity saturation) are crucial for the inverter-delay td. These parameters together with load capacitance CL and input slope sin form the parameter vector ⃖⃗p.

SSTA is an important topic in the design of integrated circuits especially in the nanometer regime. The quality of SSTA algorithms strongly depends on the underlying statistical gate-delay models. Only with accurate models it is expected that such algorithms are accepted in industry. This paper demonstrates the novel modeling technique Copulas to combine any type of statistical parameter distribution with arbitrary dependencies. The effect of different dependencies and parameter distributions has been extensively studied with a CMOS inverter. The reported results have shown significant differences concerning the inverter delay distribution and consequently the path delay distribution as shown by a simple inverter chain. Future work includes the analysis of other cells and the application of copulas to path based SSTA algorithms.
