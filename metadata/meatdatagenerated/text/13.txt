Keywords: Delay estimation Machine learning Tree-based algorithms Post-placement delay estimation Cell delay

Nowadays, the ASIC design is increasing in complexity, and PPA targets are pushed to the limit. The lack of physical information at the early design stages hinders precise timing predictions and may lead to design re-spins. In previous work, we successfully improved timing prediction at the post-placement stage using the Random Forest model, achieving 91.25% cell delay accuracy. Building upon this, we further investigate the potential of Ensemble Tree-based algorithms, specifically focusing on ‘‘Extremely Randomized Trees’’ and ‘‘Gradient Boosting’’, to close the gap in cell delay accuracy. In this paper, we enrich the training dataset with new 16 nm industrial designs. The results demonstrate a substantial improvement, with an average cell delay accuracy of 92.01% and 84.26% on unseen data. The average Root-Mean-Square-Error is significantly reduced from 12.11 to 3.23 and 7.76 on unseen data.

Recently, there has been a growing focus on achieving high timing accuracy during early design stages. The behavior of post-placement gate-level timing becomes particularly crucial as it directly affects the subsequent place-and-route stages. Timing inaccuracies during the placement stage can have significant implications on static timing analysis (STA), potentially leading to false reports of timing violations and subsequent functional failures. These inaccuracies can result in sub-optimal clock network designs, excessive power consumption and clock skew. As per the routing process, the delay inaccuracy may lead to lengthened or shortened wires, thus impacting design power and causing design re-spin as well as increasing design costs. Achieving timing closure in the early stages is crucial and various pre-placement timing optimization techniques are employed to enhance timing and alleviate timing critical paths. These techniques encompass logic reconstruction, timing-driven synthesis, technology mapping, gate-sizing, buffering, and clock-gating, among others, all aim to reduce delays on critical paths and ensure compliance with timing constraints [1–3]. Nevertheless, these techniques are effective in improving timing at the current design stage, however, they may not fully considering the impact on subsequent stages and overall design performance. 2. Related work

Machine learning has increasingly been applied to timing prediction, as evidenced by recent research. This section reviews various learning -based strategies for timing improvement, differentiating our method from existing state-of-the-art approaches. [6] developed a learning models using ANN, SVR, and Random Forest to rectify timing discrepancies in 28 nm and 45 nm designs, the study aims to reduce timing divergences in flip-flop setup time, cell arc delay, wire delay, stage delay, and path slack at timing endpoints. [7] introduced models employing Lasso, Neural Networks, and Random Forest for pre-routing timing prediction, significantly reducing timing pessimism in 45 nm designs’ net delays. The models were validated on 20 circuit implementations, with up to 45K nets. [8] focused on reducing net delay divergence and slew uncertainty using a learningbased model to correlate with signoff STA tool estimations. The model, trained on features like slew transition time, actual/required arrival

times, and cell/wire delay, was tested on designs ranging from 30K to 150K instances, demonstrating potential in mitigating misestimations. In [9], the study explores a predictive timing model, specifically correlating GBA and PBA results. It uses CART to predict PBA arrival times and slacks, utilizing a dataset of 1.7 million samples with 13 features from GBA. The dataset comprises in-house artificial designs and five medium-sized real designs. The research highlighted that dropping one feature led to a 2% drop in accuracy, while dropping two features resulted in a 4% decrease, indicating that incorporating more features could enhance accuracy. Our study aims to find a balance between maximizing the number of features and minimizing runtime, thus achieving a lower runtime with a higher number of features. [10] introduces a learning-based approach for detecting memory timing failures from the pre-placement stage, utilizing models like Lasso, SVM, Gradient Boosting, ANN, and HSM to predict post-layout slack. The study analyzed 7 small-sized 28 nm netlists, each not exceeding 200K instances, and training models on 20 features derived from netlists, floorplans, and constraints. [11] predicts crosstalk-prone nets, coupling capacitance, crosstalk noise, and incremental delay using algorithms such as LR, ANN, RF, and XGBoost to improve design routability and timing at the routing stage, focusing on 12 benchmark designs at the 7 nm, with instances ranging from 3.5K to 74K. The models, trained on 20 net features including topology, wire delay, slew, and electrical/logical aspects like capacitance and fan metrics, found XGBoost to be the most accurate, achieving around 87% accuracy in incremental delay prediction. In [12], the author developed a model to improve switching power estimation using back-annotated RC parasitics from SPEF files, aiming for a ‘‘Spefless flow’’ that predicts cell switching power without relying on SPEF files. The model was trained with 80% of data from a single test, which might limit its generalization across different designs. Utilizing Linear Regression, Random Forest, and Decision Tree algorithms, the model, despite limited training data, showed high accuracy and significantly minimized errors in switching power predictions. In [13], a pre-routing timing prediction employing Graph Neural Networks (GNN) was developed to enhance net delay and pin arrival times without the need for external router and timer engines. The study utilized 21 benchmarks from the 130 nm library, dividing them into 14 for training and 7 for testing, with a total training dataset of 920K samples, achieving an average accuracy of 94%. Similarly, [14] developed an ANN and SVM-based models to predict slew, incremental delay, and path delay related to Signal Integrity (SI), tackling timing discrepancies between SI-mode and non-SI-mode. The models were trained on a dataset of 6 designs, with instance sizes ranging from 6.5K to 350K. [15] emphasizes the impact of timing optimization on the pre-routing timing estimations. The model introduced combines Graph Neural Network (GNN) and Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to integrate layout and netlist information. The GNN model extracts and aggregates netlist information for each endpoint, whereas the CNN discerns specific layout characteristics for each endpoint. This strategy allows the net-cell delays prediction following post-timing optimization and netlist restructuring, including after modifications and re-labeling. The prediction accuracy is assessed by comparing it the sign-off delays, achieving an average R2 score of 87%. [16] introduced a post-placement net delay predictor that generates a look-ahead RC network. This Random Forest-based model is trained on cell and netrelated features to accurately predict parasitic resistance, capacitance, net delays, and critical path slack. The results show a strong correlation with pre-route sign-off STA results. [17] addressed gate delay discrepancies between single-input switching (SIS) and multi-input switching (MIS). Employing Polynomial Regression, SVR, and ANN, the study trained models on gate attributes such as output load and slew. The models were then integrated into the timing library for timing corrections. The ANN model has significantly reduced delay misestimations from 120% in SIS to 3%.

3.5. Model features

Compared to our previous study, we have reduced the number of relevant cell-net features from 101 to 45, as most of the cell and net extracted attributes were not contributing during model prediction. The current study is conducted on a reduced number of features, this will help by gaining much training and inference runtime.

The collected datasets comprise a variety of categorical input variables, we have performed feature encoding by converting categorical columns into numerical integers and boolean types to integers.

Machine Learning provides the capacity to unveil correlations among patterns and detect trends within intricate data structures. Our objective is to utilize ML techniques to unveil the relationships between the extracted cell delay and various other cell attributes. The predicted cell delay is a continuous numerical float value, which requires the use of supervised regression models. In our previous work [18], we applied various state-of-the-art regression models, including Linear Regression, Decision Tree, Random Forest, Support Vector Regression, and K-Nearest Neighbors. After evaluating their performance, we found that Random Forest had the highest predictive score.

As an example, the Arc Sense and Arc Type columns have a limited number of distinct values. We opted to treat these columns as enumerate type. Which involved creating new integer-type columns for each distinct value. The number of such additional columns corresponds to the count of distinct states. As a result, the dataframes result in a composition of 34 integer columns, 11 floating-point columns, and successful conversion of all categorical types into integers.

3.6. Dataset refinement

Our current work introduces several advancements. Firstly, we expanded our exploration by applying ensemble methods Tree-based algorithms, extending the comparison beyond our previous bestperforming model, the Random Forest. Secondly, we enhanced our analysis by incorporating two additional larger designs. Furthermore, in this study, we significantly refined the extracted features to a subset of 45 cell and net features. Finally, we tested the model’s generalization capability using larger design (TC7). Table 1 delineates the size of each design in terms of the number of standard cells, highlighting the increased complexity and scale of the designs considered in this study.

As a crucial step in data pre-processing, the removal of outliers is imperative to ensure the robustness and reliability of the dataset. Outliers refer to data points that deviate significantly from the majority of the dataset, often caused by miscalculations or other anomalies. In our approach, we began by plotting scatter plots for several floatingtype attributes, including cell output capacitance, input slew fall, output slew fall, input slew rise, output slew rise, delay rise, and delay fall. These scatter plots allow us to visually identify any data points that appear as outliers.

The selected tree-based models include ‘Extremely Randomized Forests (Extra-Trees)’, ‘Gradient Boosting (GB)’, ‘Light Gradient Boosting (LightGBM)’, ‘eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost)’, and ‘Adaptive Boosting (AdaBoost)’ [21–23]. These algorithms are decision tree based structures, it partitions the inputs into regions. The internal nodes corresponds to features and each branch is called a decision. Each decision assigns a predicted value to each leaf fof the tree [24–26]. The Gradient Boosting techniques are built upon weak decision tree learners, it minimizes the errors of previous decision gradually until refining the overall model through the combination of weak learners. However, the Extra Trees (Extremely Randomized Trees) are ensemble methods that randomly select feature subsets for node and introduces additional randomness to mitigate variance and potential overfitting within the model [27,28].

Subsequently, we employed three different methods for outlier removal: ‘‘Z-score’’, ‘‘DBSCAN’’ and ‘‘Interquartile Range (IQR)’’. The focus is to get the highest accuracy while minimizing the number of removed outliers from the dataset. We have fine-tuned each method to identify the threshold beyond which the data is considered an outlier. We implemented Z-score at 3-4-5 standard deviation from the mean. Similarly, for IQR, we explored different quartile ranges, such as 75- 25, 85-15, and 95-5, to identify the range at which outliers became prominent. We utilized the Interquartile Range (IQR) approach with a 4-sigma cutoff. This allowed us to establish up and low boundaries based on the mean value. Cells that fell beyond these bounds were considered outliers and subsequently discarded. Through iterative analysis, we discovered that the optimal approach involves initially applying the IQR method with a range of 85-15 to identify and discard the initial set of outliers that exceed the 4-sigma threshold. Subsequently, we proceeded to apply the Z-score method at the 4th standard deviation. Table 4 provides a summary of the percentage of outliers for each design.

To evaluate the quality of predictions, we utilize Root-Mean- Squared-Error and R2-score metrics. The RMSE function serves to assess the performance of each model by calculating the average magnitude of the errors produced, thereby providing a measure of the overall error rate. It is calculated by taking the square root of the mean of the squared differences between the predicted values and the actual values. A lower RMSE indicates better accuracy of the model. (Eq. (1)). Additionally, we employ the R2-score to evaluate the overall accuracy of the predictions. The R2 score, also known as the coefficient of determination, measures the variance in the target variable. A higher R2 score represents a more accurate alignment of the model predictions with the target values (Eq. (2)).
