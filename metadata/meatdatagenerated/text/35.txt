Adjacency criticality: a simple yet effective metric for statistical timing yield optimisation of digital integrated circuits

Abstract: As CMOS devices become smaller, process variations-induced uncertainty imposes a large spread in the circuit timing and therefore, it becomes one of the main issues for circuit yield. To analyse/optimise the timing of the circuit under process variation effects, statistical analysis/optimisation techniques are more suitable than the traditional static analysis/ optimisation counterparts. Statistical gate sizing is an effective technique that is widely used to guide the timing yield improvement of digital circuits. Gate criticality, defined as the probability that a gate lies on a critical path, forms the basis for many of the existing statistical gate sizing techniques. Here, the authors introduce adjacency criticality to address the drawbacks of the conventional definition of gate criticality. It is defined as the probability of manufacturing a chip in which the gate lies on the critical path due to process variation considering the effect of the gates in its fan-out cone. Furthermore, the authors present the levelised Adjacency Criticality metric which provides a trade-off between the runtime of the criticality metric and accuracy of the Adjacency Criticality metric. In order to show the efficacy of the proposed metric, an adjacency criticalitybased statistical gate sizing method is presented for improving timing yield of the circuit.

1௑Introduction

In order to gain more advances in different aspects such as performance and power consumption, integrated circuits manufacturing continuously has followed a trend of transistor dimensions shrinking [1]. As technology continues to scale down, the difficulty of precise fabrication process increases. This causes fabrication process-induced parameter variations (briefly process variations) such as changes in gate oxide thickness and threshold voltage which significantly affect the circuit timing and performance [2]. Process variations may change the circuit frequency up to 30% [3]. Thus, the gap between the designed layout and what is really fabricated on silicon is widening significantly. Handling the process variations effects at design time has the advantage of taking the right decisions early in the design flow which in turn, significantly reduces the time-to-market of the design. As a result, design-time compensating of process variations impacts is an inevitable requirement for modern digital circuits. Deterministic timing analysis has been widely used to handle variability effects in very large-scale integrated (VLSI) circuit designs [4, 5]. However, this approach loses accuracy in analysing circuits under considerable process variations in nanoscale era. This is due to the fact that it estimates nominal, best-case, or worstcase delay of a circuit; best-case (worst-case) analysis significantly underestimates (overestimates) the process variations effects, and analysing circuits at nominal values of various circuit parameters completely ignores these variations [4, 5]. As a promising alternative, statistical analysis which is based on estimating the distribution performance of the circuit metrics using probability theory and statistics has shown more accurate results in variabilityaware performance evaluation of digital circuits. Statistical static timing analysis (SSTA) is a widely used method for timing analysis in digital circuits in the presence of process variations. There are many SSTA methods that trade-off between time complexity and accuracy [6–11]. Statistical timing optimisation methods take advantages of SSTAs as the basis in order to improve the performance/yield of a digital circuit considering the impacts of process variations [12]. Hence, the efficacy of the statistical timing optimisation method is directly affected by the quality of the

SSTAs as well as the information provided by the circuit analyser to be applied in the optimisation method [13]. In order to obtain specialised information for steering statistical optimisation, characteristic metrics have been introduced [6, 13– 35]. Yield gradient (YG) which is an effective sensitivity metric for guiding the statistical timing optimisation flow is defined as the change in the timing yield (TY) of the circuit for small changes in the size of the gates [19, 24]. To assess the change in TY of the circuit, an SSTA is performed to obtain the change in the circuit delay due to a change in the size of the gates; i.e. YG is evaluated numerically. In [24], using a YG computation method, a gate sizing algorithm for parametric yield maximisation is introduced. Although YG is an accurate metric, YG-based optimisation algorithms are intrinsically iterative, and in each iteration, the YG of a large number of gates needs to be evaluated in order to designate the best candidate for resizing. Another metric, TY slack (YS) is introduced in [13]. The YS represents the extra TY available in a gate or a circuit. However, in order to compute YS of each gate, a large number of gates needs to be evaluated which is time-consuming for even mid-scale digital circuits. In [20, 22], an indicator named as statistical timing criticality (STC) is introduced that represents the probability that the gate lies on the critical path considering process variations. STC is used for TY optimisation by improving the delay of the gates which have high STC values. In [25], the criticality of a path is expressed as the sensitivity of the mean of the normally distributed random variable of circuit delay to the mean of the path delay. In [6], a fast method to compute the criticality of the path and the node/arc is presented. This method is valid under the assumption that the paths are independent. However, due to correlated parameters and path reconvergence, it is not sufficiently accurate to be applied for TY optimisation of large and complicated circuits. Zhan et al. [26] compute the path criticality based on max operations and Monte– Carlo simulations. In this method, using a pre-selected path set, paths are picked out for criticality computation. However, this method suffers from a serious drawback, i.e. it is not clear how to determine the pre-selected path set (like path-based SSTA). Xiong et al. [27] introduced a cut-set-based method to compute criticality. After identifying all the cut set of the timing graph, the node/edge criticality is defined as the probability of the node/edge slack (the

In order to compute the delay distribution of the circuit, timing graph of the circuit is created. The timing graph is a directed acyclic graph G(V, E) composed of nodes (V) and edges (E). The nodes represent different input pins of circuit gates. They are connected using edges that model signal propagation from one node to another with an associated delay demonstrating either gate or wire delay. Considering PV effects, this delay is a random variable with a specific probability density function (PDF). The timing graph is augmented by a single source node and a single sink node. The source node is a virtual node which is connected to all primary inputs via virtual edges and the virtual sink node is a virtual node which is connected to all primary outputs or timing test nodes with virtual edges. Given the circuit timing graph and the statistical gate delay model as inputs, SSTA computes the statistical distribution of the circuit delay with linear runtime complexity [11]. In this paper, we apply the widely used canonical first-order delay model [6] with a relatively low complexity. In order to overcome the error induced by max operator in criticality computation, a criticality computation method using unary and conditional operator is introduced in [28]. In [22, 29], an incremental criticality computation using circuit delay is introduced. In order to reduce the power consumption of the circuit, [31, 32] propose a dual supply voltage (DSV) technique in which a lower supply voltage is applied to gates such that the performance of the circuit maintains at the same time. To this end, these methods first compute the criticality of each gate. Then, DSV is applied to a group of the gates with the lowest criticality. At the presence of false paths, i.e. paths through which signals cannot propagate through it, finding the true critical paths is so difficult. If the false paths are the critical paths of the circuit, analysis of the circuit using SSTA would be too optimistic. Furthermore, criticality computation of the circuit would be inaccurate. Sreenath and Ramesh [33] propose a gate sizing method in which they first identify the false path of the circuit. Then, a group of gates selected from the true critical paths according to their criticality and gate sizing is applied to them. In order to lessen the impact of process variation, post-silicon clock tuning elements are widely used in high-performance circuits. These elements can be configured through the scan chain so that clock skews to these flip-flops can be adjusted after manufacturing. Moreover, since these elements allow the path delays to compensate each other across flip-flops, critical paths in such circuits may span more than one stage which makes the criticality computation of such a circuit so challenging. In [34], a SSTA method is introduced to compute the performance of the circuit under post-silicon clock tuning elements. Furthermore, they propose a criticality computation method in these circuits so that an analysing-optimisation flow becomes possible. In order to optimise the delay of the circuit, critical paths or nodes are the first targets in applying the circuit optimisation technique. Thus, it is important to select the critical gates correctly. However, all of the previous optimisation methods using criticality are suffering from a serious drawback. They did not consider the effect of the gates in the fan-out cone of each gate. Since optimising the delay of gate g will affect the arrival time (AT) of the gates in its fan-out cone, and as the criticality of the gates in the fan-out cone of a gate become higher, the effect of optimising that gate on the circuit delay become higher, neglecting the effect of the fan-out gates will give a more pessimistic (optimistic) criticality which slows down the convergence rate of the optimisation algorithm and result in high area overhead. In this paper, we propose a novel optimisation metric called adjacency criticality (AC). It is defined as the probability of manufacturing a chip in which the gate lies on the critical path, due to process variation considering the effect of gates in its fan-out cone. In order to show the efficacy of the proposed metric, an ACbased statistical gate sizing method is presented for improving TY of the circuit. Experimental results on EPFL and ISCAS benchmark circuits show that the proposed metric leads to faster convergence of the optimisation algorithm and lower area overhead compared to the criticality metric. Due to process variation effects, path delays are no longer deterministic values, but are described by random variable with a specific PDF. This implies that each of the paths can be critical with some probability, and none of the paths can be critical with absolute certainty; i.e. we have a set of critical paths instead of only a critical path. Since the critical path set changes from die to die due to process variations effects, computing criticality is a crucial step in TY optimisation. As defined in [21], ‘Criticality of a path is the probability of manufacturing a chip in which the path of interest is critical’. ‘Criticality of a gate is defined as the probability of manufacturing a chip in which the gate lies on the critical path, due to process variation’[21]. In other words, the criticality of a gate is the criticality of the set of all paths going through that gate. Using criticality, critical paths or gates become the key nominees to apply the circuit optimisation techniques [27]. 4௑Timing yield optimisation using adjacency criticality

The proposed algorithm of AC computation is implemented in C++ and run on a Microsoft Windows machine with an Intel Core i7 (2.4 GHz) processor and an 8 GB of RAM circuit. All of the gates in the circuit are assigned with a base size, i.e. the W/L ratio of PMOS in an inverter is set to 2 and W/L ratio for its NMOS is set to 1. In addition, for other gates (NAND, NOR etc.), we have sized the gates such that they have pull-up and pull-down networks with equal current strengths. In this paper, we assume process variations in effective channel length and threshold voltage. However, one could consider other source of variations; for example in the channel width and oxide thickness of the transistor. All of the random variables are set to the standard Gaussian random variable N ∼ (0, 1). The experiments are divided into two parts. At first, we compare the AC concept with the criticality concept using various constraints. Then, in the second part, we investigate the efficiency of the proposed gate sizing optimisation algorithm using AC concept by applying the algorithm to some benchmark circuits.

6௑Conclusion

Diagnostic metrics are the cornerstone of optimisation to provide valuable information for guiding statistical optimisation algorithms. In this paper, we introduce a novel optimisation metric called AC. Then, using the proposed metric, a gate sizing-based optimisation algorithm is presented that aims to improve the TY of a given circuit. As discussed in [29], a change in the size of a gate increases the load capacitance seen by the gates in its fan-in cone. Therefore, it increases their delay and output slew. So, as a future extension of our work, the effect of fan-in gates on the AC of a gate should be taken into account. It is worthy to note that in this paper, we have assumed that the gates and circuit timing follow Gaussian distribution and thus, we use Gaussian distribution-based criticality computation method. However, the proposed AC metric is independent of the type of the distribution of random variables and one could use criticality computation methods presented in the [19, 21, 37] for computing criticality in digital circuits that does not follow the Gaussian distribution since these methods does not depend on any specific distributions or any particular delay models.
