Keywords:  
To evaluate digital circuit performance across PVT (process, voltage, and temperature) corners, standard  
cell library characterization requires costly simulations. Leveraging machine learning (ML) can improve the  
efficiency of this process. However, existing ML-based methods for cell library characterization often neglect the  
knowledge embedded across different timing arcs, leading to the need for extensive training data. In this paper,  
we propose a transfer learning (TL) framework to enhance timing characterization across multiple timing arcs.  
By quantifying the similarity among training tasks in the cell library using a fine-grained metric, our method  
enables rapid and accurate cell delay predictions through pre-training knowledge. Compared to conventional  
ML approaches, our TL framework improves both prediction accuracy and efficiency. Experimental results on  
45 nm MOSFET and 14 nm FINFET technologies show significant error reductions of up to 80% and 67%,  
respectively.

To address the exploration of pre-training knowledge among library  
cells, in this paper, we propose a knowledge transferring framework. In  
the framework, we first characterize various timing values for library

14 nm FINFET technologies across various PVT corners. And then the

The key contributions are as follows:  
In the modern integrated circuit designs, standard cell libraries  
serve as the fundamental building blocks for the entire design and op-  
timization process [1,2]. To evaluate the performance of standard cell  
under different work conditions, library characterization is essential.  
Considering traditional characterization process involves the utilization  
of numerous SPICE simulations, there are two challenges need to be  
addressed. On the one hand, industry-standard cell libraries typically  
comprise hundreds to thousands of cell types [3], encompassing various  
logical functions and drive strengths. On the other hand, as technology  
nodes shrink to nanometer scales, PVT increasingly affect chip per-  
formance [4–6]. This results in a significant increase in PVT corners  
with technology scaling, especially exceeding 1000 when considering  
process variations [7]. Therefore, the combination of aforementioned  
characterization requirements will result in an exponential increase in  
simulation runtime.  
∙ We use a fine-grained Wasserstein distance-based metric to assess  
similarity across hundreds of learning tasks, and demonstrate the  
positive guiding effect of this metric on TL.  
∙ We utilize effective TL algorithm by augmenting features for  
ensemble regression models. Furthermore, we evaluate their ef-  
fectiveness across various underlying models, including SVR, RF,  
and PRR. Compared to PRR, our proposed framework shows a  
significant reduction in prediction errors, up to 80%, and achieves  
up to a 1.96× reduction in required simulation costs.  
In recent years, many ML-based methods are introduced to ac-  
celerate the generation of cell library. The underlying ML models  
include random forest (RF) [8], support vector regression (SVR) [9],  
feed-forward neural network (FFNN) [10], polynomial ridge regression  
(PRR) [11–15]. In particular, existing works [8–15] focus on train-  
ing predictive models for individual timing arcs within library cells.

current ML-based methods do not fully explore the embedded knowl-

2.1. ML-based cell library characterization  
First of all, it is important to note that cell delay is a critical factor in  
determining the overall performance of a circuit. Therefore, this work  
primarily focuses on the delay characterization of the cell library.  
The standard cell library comprises various cells with multiple logi-  
cal functions and drive strengths. Due to variations in cell performance  
under different input conditions, each cell requires simulation for dif-  
ferent timing arcs. For each timing arc, the results are represented using  
look-up tables (LUTs) across different indexes, serving as fundamental  
components in the standard cell library. The number of components is  
typically in the thousands [12].  
3. Proposed framework  
The overview of our proposed framework is illustrated in Fig. 3.  
As shown in this figure, three main steps are defined. In the first step,  
simulation samples are generated to create training and testing datasets  
(see Section 3.1). Next, baseline models are trained using an ensemble  
bagging strategy (see Section 3.2). Finally, based on task grouping  
methods, we apply feature-based TL algorithm to explore embedded  
knowledge and enhance accuracy for baseline models (see Section 3.3).  
When using ML methods to characterize the standard cell library,  
modeling each basic component with LUT-formed data can be regarded  
as an individual learning task. As shown in Fig. 1, the traditional  
approaches involve generating LUT data for various timing arcs to  
create simulation datasets. Each simulation dataset is then treated as  
an independent learning task for fitting individual models [8–15]. To  
achieve accurate predictions, traditional methods rely on increasing the  
number of simulations to expand the training dataset.

Furthermore, our proposed TL methods is scalable to multiple under-

Section 4.

2.2. Transfer learning (TL)  
Traditional ML techniques heavily depend on available training  
data, where the quality and quantity play critical roles in the training  
process. Limited or inaccurate training data can significantly degrade  
prediction performance. Generating missing data directly for the target  
task is challenging, but leveraging knowledge from a related domain  
can be valuable [16]. TL offers an effective approach to transferring  
knowledge between different domains or tasks, particularly beneficial  
for improving ML model performance when labeled data is costly [17].  
Typically, the task and sample domain of interest are referred to as the  
target task and domain, while similar tasks and sample domains are  
known as source tasks and domains.  
Each timing arc in the cell library need to be characterized under  
every PVT corner. The features (corner-related parameters) and labels  
(LUT-formed cell delays) are stored in specific domains. Given a cell  
library for technology ꢁ containing ꢃ timing arcs, we denote the  
domain corresponding to the ꢄth timing arc ꢅꢄ as D(ꢁ)ꢅꢄ . For training  
the following baseline models and performing TL optimization, the  
features and labels (illustrated in model training part of Fig. 1) from  
these domains will serve as the inputs and ground truth for prediction  
models.

tasks within the cell library, in this work, we utilize the feature-based

3.2.1. Baseline model construction  
The baseline model refers to the model obtained by directly training  
on the training set using a specific underlying model. Since TL is  
essentially the training optimizing process of the baseline model, the  
quality of the baseline model significantly influences the final pre-  
diction accuracy. In our proposed framework, the ensemble bagging  
learning strategy is adopted to construct a high-quality baseline model.  
The adopted ensemble bagging learning strategy involves training  
multiple instances of the same underlying model on different subsets  
of the training data, employing a random selection with replacement  
(known as bootstrapping). In addition, the sampling ratio is set to  
0.8, indicating that each subset comprises 80% of samples from the  
training data. Finally, the baseline regression prediction is obtained by  
averaging the predictions of these sub-models. This approach enables  
the improvement of the overall performance of the model by reducing  
overfitting and increasing robustness. As illustrated in the step 2 in  
Fig. 3, balancing efficiency and accuracy considerations, 3 sub-models  
are separately trained for each learning task.

3.2.2. Underlying ML model implementation  
We define the underlying model as the type of ML model used  
for the sub-models. The learning task for cell delay prediction is a  
typical regression task. And different types of underlying ML models  
including RF [8], SVR [9], FFNN [10], and PRR [11–15] are employed  
to solve this regression task. Based on our experiments, configuring the  
optimal structure and fitting the extensive model parameters for each  
task using the FFNN model requires significant runtime. Furthermore,  
the predictive accuracy of the FFNN model is not optimal (more details  
can be found in Section 4.2). Therefore, three other ML models (RF,  
SVR, and PRR) are chosen as underlying models to demonstrate the  
effectiveness of our proposed framework.  
In order to train and optimize hyperparameters for different under-  
lying models, the features in the training set are initially normalized to  
[0, 1] using the min–max normalization method, based on the upper  
and lower bounds of the feature ranges listed in Table 1. Following this,  
grid search is performed using 5-fold cross-validation on the training  
set. The hyperparameters searched for our implemented underlying  
models are summarized in Table 2. By tuning the optimal hyperpa-  
rameters for each individual underlying model, three sub-models are  
fitted on subsets bootstrapped from the training set. Finally, as defined  
in Eq. (1), all sub-models are aggregated into a baseline model for each  
timing arc.

or labels of grouped tasks are similar, the model can adapt more  
efficiently to the target task, potentially requiring less labeled data  
for training [17]. Therefore, assessing and maximizing similarity are  
essential considerations in the implementation of TL models, as they  
directly impact the ability of the model to generalize across different

4. Experiment and evaluation results  
The previous work [15] introduces a similarity evaluation method  
based on edit distance and Hamming distance. It considers that the  
smaller the difference among Boolean functions of timing arcs, the  
more similar are the learning tasks of timing arcs. However, this is  
an intuition-based and coarse-grained metric. For the demand of cell  
library characterization with a large number of different learning tasks,  
a theoretical and fine-grained similarity evaluation metric is required.

4.1. Experimental setup  
To validate the effectiveness of our proposed framework, we con-  
duct experiments on 14 nm FINFET and 45 nm MOSFET cell li-  
braries [19,20] across various PVT corners listed in Table 1. The  
tested cells constitute a shared subset of library cells with identical  
logical functions and drive strengths from both the Nangate 14 nm  
and Nangate 45 nm libraries. As summarized in Table 3, 48 cells  
are chosen for testing in each technology. According to varying input  
conditions across different cells in each technology, we need to model  
a total of 213 timing arcs to predict 799 cell delay types. In other  
words, our framework will be executed once for every timing arc.  
To prepare training data for each timing arc, we simulate cell delays  
based on a 7 × 7 index dimension. During the training process for each  
timing arc, 30% of the samples from the task-specific sample pool are  
randomly selected as the test set, while the remaining 70% constitute  
the complete training set. For testing with different training sizes,  
additional samples are randomly selected from the full training set and  
combined with the initial 100 samples used for distance computation to  
create the actual available training set. Moreover, the available training  
sets selected in the source domain and the target domain through the  
aforementioned process serve as the inputs Dꢏ and Dꢐ for Algorithm 1,  
respectively.

4.2.3. Performance and generalization evaluation  
In addition, we conduct an ablation study on our proposed bagging  
ensemble and TL strategies. As presented in Table 4, when using com-  
plete training set and testing on the baseline model with PRR, both the  
bagging and TL techniques independently contribute to performance  
improvements. Notably, the combination of all techniques yields the  
most substantial enhancement, achieving a remarkable RMSE reduction  
of 82% and 69% for the 45 nm and 14 nm libraries, respectively. Fur-  
thermore, by averaging the results of timing arcs within cells sharing  
the same logic function (disregarding differences in input number and  
drive strength) and subsequently averaging across all 213 tested timing  
arcs, we observe that the performance gain achieved through the TL  
strategy is significantly more pronounced compared to that achieved  
through the bagging strategy. This indicates that the TL method plays  
a crucial role in enhancing model prediction performance.  
4.2.4. Efficiency comparisons  
By leveraging an efficient TL strategy, we can reduce the number of  
required simulations and overall learning runtime needed to achieve  
a specific accuracy goal. Experimenting with PRR for optimal perfor-  
mance, Fig. 9 shows the trends in the amount of training samples and  
RMSEs for the baseline model and the TL model. To achieve the best  
accuracy of the baseline model, our TL strategy reduces the required  
simulation costs by 1.96× and 1.75× for the 45 nm and 14 nm libraries,  
respectively.  
Moreover, based on the best RMSEs achieved by the PRR model  
on the largest training set, as shown in Fig. 9, we compare the total  
runtime of the PRR model with that of our proposed TL model. These  
results are summarized in Table 5. For the PRR model, the training  
runtime includes data processing/loading, model training, and valida-  
tion. The training runtime of the TL model additionally accounts for the  
similarity evaluation and TL process. However, the variation in training  
runtime is negligible compared to the training data preparation time  
required for SPICE simulations. Using the trained model for inference,  
our approach significantly reduces the runtime for generating all 48  
libraries listed in Table 1, achieving a 63% reduction for the Nangate  
45 nm library and 60% for the Nangate 14 nm library.  
Furthermore, to demonstrate the generalization performance of our  
proposed transfer learning method, we test the trained models using the  
complete training set sampled from the feature space in Table 1. These  
models are then used to predict cell delays under unseen PVT corners.  
Specifically, in addition to the voltage and temperature conditions  
listed in Table 1, we sample additional voltage conditions with steps  
of 50 mV for the 45 nm library and 40 mV for the 14 nm library,  
resulting in unseen voltage conditions of [1.0, 1.05, 1.15, 1.20] V and  
[0.74, 0.78, 0.82, 0.86] V, respectively. Moreover, additional temperature  
conditions [−20, 75] ◦C are sampled. Combined with all the voltage  
and temperature conditions, we construct 30 unseen PVT corners (with  
process fixed as SS in Table 1).  
In summary, our framework effectively utilizes embedded knowl-  
edge across various timing arcs to enhance prediction performance and  
reduce overall runtime.

4.3. Model inference for STA  
To demonstrate the efficiency of our cell libraries for static tim-  
ing analysis (STA), we use models trained on 70% of the Nangate  
14 nm dataset, as discussed in Section 4.2.1, to generate libraries  
for all 48 PVT corners listed in Table 1. The critical path delays ob-  
tained from generated libraries are compared against the golden results  
derived from simulated libraries. Additionally, we evaluate the perfor-  
mance on five circuits from EPFL benchmarks [24]. As shown Fig. 10,  
when comparing with other methods such as SVR [9], RF [8], and  
PRR [13,15], the relative errors of critical path delays based on our  
framework-generated libraries are mostly concentrated within 5%.  
Furthermore, the gates of critical paths in different circuits and  
average absolute relative error are given in the Table 6. We can observe  
that the prediction relative errors vary across different critical path  
structures, with worst case showing errors exceeding 20%. Although  
PRR [13,15] demonstrates overall performance better than SVR [9]  
and RF [8] models with an average error of 5.2%, using our proposed  
framework further reduces this error to 2.3%. Finally, we compare the  
runtime of our model inference for single cell library generation with  
simulation. Generating a single library takes 6.7 s, achieving a 134×  
speedup compared to the simulation process, which requires 15 min  
for characterization. This demonstrates that our framework enables  
on-the-fly cell library generation.
