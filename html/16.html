Multicorner Timing Analysis Acceleration for Iterative Physical Design of ICs

Wei W. Xing , Member, IEEE, Longze Wang, Student Member, IEEE, Zhelong Wang, Student Member, IEEE, Zhaoyu Shi, Student Member, IEEE, Ning Xu, Member, IEEE, Yuanqing Cheng , Senior Member, IEEE, and Weisheng Zhao , Fellow, IEEE

Abstract. We propose a multicorner multistage timing analysis prediction framework using a generalized linear model with latent features. We then further improve such methods using kernel trick extension, transfer learning with knowledge from previous designs, and multioutput feature engineering to deliver state-of-the-art (SOTA) prediction accuracy with very limited training data. Most importantly, our method is equipped with a Bayesian decision strategy to deliver reliable predictions with accuracy close to 100%, pushing the frontier of the machine-learning-based static timing analysis (STA) for practical implementation in the industry environment, where reliability is highly desired. Experimental results show that the accuracy of our proposed method outperforms the SOTA competitors by up to 4x and can improve prediction accuracy to 100% with little extra STA executions.

I. INTRODUCTION I N ORDER to guarantee the timing yield, chip designers resort to static timing analysis (STA) to evaluate the timing correctness under different process corners (PCs) [1]. Thanks to the fast speed and reliability of STA, it has been widely used for timing verification in the past few decades. As the technology node scales down into the nanometer regime, the process, voltage, and temperature (PVT) variations impact chip performance significantly [2]. As a result, the number of process corners increases drastically with the technology node scaling [3], [4]. Even worse, the conventional physical design goes through synthesis, floorplan, power plan, placement, clock synthesis, routing, and engineering change order (ECO) optimizations. Each stage requires extensive timing simulations to pinpoint the timing failure as early as possible. Therefore, STA has become an ever-increasing time-consuming procedure in modern chip timing verification. To reduce the timing analysis overhead, several effective techniques have been proposed, which can be mainly classified into two categories. One is to reduce the time to that of one process corner analysis [6], [7]. More specifically, Silva et al. [6] used a branch-and-bound method to obtain the worst-case process corner for chip sign-off timing analysis. Based on a similar idea, Onaissi and Najm [7] derived the worst-case timing results much faster than a normal multicorner STA. By estimating the worst-case timing among PCs, the upper bound of the worst timing of the critical path can be evaluated with one-pass analysis instead of running STA for all process corners. However, the upper bound may not be a strictly tight bound, leading to an overdesign with a conservatively large design margin. Moreover, the worst case may not occur in practical operational conditions because the combinations of PVT leading to the worst-case delay are less likely to occur simultaneously. Another category of accelerating STA is taking the process and environment parameters as independent random variables, and using statistical techniques for one-pass timing analysis, such as [8], [9]. Recently, inspired by the success of machine learning in EDA fields, researchers and industry have started to investigate the use of data-driven techniques to accelerate STA. Andrew et al. [10] proposed a multivariate linear regression technique to predict path timings of вЂњunobserved cornersвЂќ with path timings of вЂњobserved corners.вЂќ The intuition is that the timing result of all corners lives in a low-dimensional ambient space and thus can be reconstructed using timing results under some corners by harnessing the correlation under all corners. Following the same assumption, Zhao et al. [11] put forth nonlinear regression models for the STA prediction with improved accuracy. Also, they proposed using a statistical analysis tool to pick key STA results (referred to as dominating process corners) as model inputs to save extra STA executions while maintaining good predictive accuracy.

B. Classic STA Prediction Methods

Ideally, we would like to directly predict the STA matrix Y(z) using a machine learning model for any given circuit z. However, this is almost an impossible task because we cannot quantify z, i, nor j as model inputs, not to mention their complex interactions. To resolve this challenge, [10], [11] compromise the ultimate goal of directly predicting Y(z). Instead, they make it a matrix infill task, where the STA matrix Y(z) is partially observed, and the goal is to predict the missing entries. Denote the ith column of Y(z) as y(z, i). Their method first decides a set of IвЂІ (IвЂІ << I) PCs that matter most in the predictions and uses this as model input for a regression model. The inputs are then x = (y(z, 1, j), . . . , y(z, IвЂІ, j))вЉ¤

whereas the model target output are defined as t = (y(z, IвЂІ + 1, j), . . . , y(z, I, j))вЉ¤. This way, each row of Y(z) in sampling data D produces a model input and corresponding output pair to train a regression model. Note that the selection of the corner can be arbitrary without following an ordinal order. The goal is to approximate the mapping from the known STA results x to the unknown results t with existing data

t = g(x) (1)

Despite its success, this type of method is limited because 1) it requires many data to correctly choose its dominant process corners; 2) it assumes a simple model for all circuits, which is neither realistic nor accurate; 3) the model can not update itself when it is used in an iterative optimization process and may eventually lead to poor accuracy; 4) the dominant process corners are prefixed and cannot be adapted with more information coming into the system; 5) the model lacks the ability to tell the confidence of the prediction accuracy, which makes it unrealistic to be deployed in a practical chip design where robustness is crucial. To resolve these issues, we solve the STA prediction problem around all main physical design stages in a unique space taking advantage of their close correlations. We propose a multilinear regression model with implicit latent features to harness the historical information and predict the STA results accurately. We then generalize our model as a nonlinear model to deal with complex STA problems along with a scalable and efficient model training procedure. Besides providing more accurate prediction than traditional methods, the proposed method provides a Bayesian decision strategy based on the predictive distribution to actively update the model for uncertain predictions and improve the prediction accuracy to nearly 100% with little cost. This way, the timing predictions are given with confidence, and the results are reliable, making the proposed method more practical to be implemented in a real industrial design. Furthermore, feature engineering is added to our method to further improve the prediction accuracy. In summary, our contributions are listed as follows. 1) We propose a novel flexible framework for the STA prediction problem with latent features that enable knowledge transfer from previous experimental data. 2) We establish a Bayesian nonlinear model that is accurate, scalable, and flexible for timing prediction. 3) We put forth multioutput feature engineering (MoFe) to further improve model accuracy. 4) We proposed a Bayesian decision strategy to assist STA, enhancing the reliability of STA predictions with active correction such that the model accuracy is almost 100%, making the proposed method, more practical for real chip designs. The remainder of this article is organized as follows. Section II formulates the STA prediction problem and analyzes the drawbacks of the classic approach, which motivates this work. Section III describes the proposed method with detailed key techniques involved. Section IV illustrates the experiment results and Section V concludes our work.

We define three key indicators to examine the performance: 1) Coverage, the ratio of the wrong predictions correctly marked divided by the total number of wrong predictions (higher is better); 2) ExcessRatio, the total number of correctly and incorrectly marked paths divided by the number of wrong predictions (lower is better); and 3) ExtraCost, the total number of marked paths, which need rerun of STA for these points, divided by the initial STA runs (lower is better).
