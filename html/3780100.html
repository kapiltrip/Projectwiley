Timing closure is a critical but effort-taking task in VLSI designs. In this paper, we investigate timing models and their associated optimization techniques suitable for integration into early design process. For accurate timing analysis at placement stage, an innovative timing prediction model that can be transformed into a derivable function is proposed, serving as a replacement for the conventional Elmore delay. One significant advantage of derivable timing models is their ability to be formulated as mathematical functions. This enables the derivation of gradients, which can be explored as direct timing optimization guidance during design process. Therefore, we further present a timing optimization method through model integration into iterative placement process. Experimental results demonstrate that our derivable timing model provides accurate prediction performance. Additionally, when integrated into the placement stage, it outperforms the use of Elmore delay and non-derivable models, achieving 12%∼24% smaller Total Negative Slack (TNS) and Worst Negative Slack (WNS), with an average wirelength reduction of 1%∼5%.

1 Introduction

Timing closure is a critical yet challenging task in VLSI design. Timing optimizations are performed throughout the entire design process. The earlier the intervention stage it involves in, the greater the room it has for timing optimization. Today, many timing optimization techniques are applied at early stages of design. For instance, during the placement stage, various methods have been proposed, i.e., net-based and path-based algorithms. However, aside from modifying the process algorithms for timing-driven optimization, it has also been observed that the accuracy of the timing model can significantly impact timing performance. Accurate timing estimation before sign-off stage, i.e., at placement stage, is challenging due to the absence of essential connection physical information. As a result, traditional timing analysis methods rely on simplified circuit models, which reduce accuracy significantly as wire lengths and fan-outs grow. To address timing closure under inaccurate timing analysis, circuits are designed using a worst-case assumption, which adds an over-pessimistic timing margin to ensure the performance specification under process variations. Industrial experience has shown that, the immense gap between the performance predicted by EDA tools and the final silicon performance can be up to 30% [34]. In addition to the pessimistic timing margin, in early design stage, a large arbitrary clock uncertainty is inserted in order to cover up the net delay estimation “error” before routing. Consequently, the design might be optimized with a large mis-correlation with the sign-off net delay information, which could result in multiple design turn-around iterations and/or poor sign-off Power-Performance-Area (PPA) trade-offs. To overcome the accuracy limitations of early timing analysis, recent methods have utilized machine learning (ML) models like random forest [6, 22], XGBoost [9], and Graph Neural Network (GNN) models [20]. These techniques have shown improved timing prediction accuracy. However, a key challenge remains: integrating these accurate ML models into the design flow for practical performance evaluation. Most existing timing-driven algorithms rely on the Elmore model for timing analysis. Although the Elmore model is known to be inaccurate, it continues to be widely used in design tools for preliminary timing analysis due to its simple mathematical formula, which allows for computational efficiency and facilitates the derivation of timing optimizations in various timing-driven placement algorithms [7, 8, 11, 19, 28, 29, 32, 32, 33, 33, 35]. Therefore, to enable machine learning (ML) to provide more accurate timing optimization, ML models must not only improve their accuracy but also incorporate integration methods that can guide timing optimization effectively. In this work, we aim to enhance both accurate timing analysis and corresponding optimization guidance method for timing model in placement stage, leading to more efficient circuit designs as shown in Fig. 1. Our focus is on developing a solution that combines the accuracy of ML-based model with the computational efficiency and derivability advantages of the Elmore model. Firstly, to match the benefits of the Elmore model, we propose a novel timing prediction model based on Gradient Boosting Machine (GBM). Similar to the Elmore model, our GBM model can be transformed into a derivable function. To enhance model accuracy, we generate a lookahead RC network during the placement stage for essential timing feature extraction. To further assess the effectiveness of our proposed timing model, we give the model integration method. As the model derivation can identify timing benefits related to connection criticality, the critical path can be optimized based on the partial derivative of our GBM model with respect to wirelength. In our implementation, the GBM model is integrated into the open-source placer DREAMPlace [30], employing net-weighting technology as a study case for timing optimization evaluation. Our experiments demonstrate that, compared to previous net-weighting methods, our model and its integration significantly reduce unnecessary wirelength adjustments during timing optimization, leading to superior Total Negative Slack (TNS) and Worst Negative Slack (WNS) results. The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:

It is observed that the Elmore model simplifies delay calculation by expressing it directly as a function of resistance Rand capacitanceC, both of which have a linear relationship with wirelength. This allows timing-driven placement algorithms to translate the optimization of critical path delay into the optimization of wirelength. For example, net-weighting methods [8, 28, 29, 35] assign higher weights to timing-critical nets during placement. This prioritizes shorter wirelengths for these critical connections, ultimately reducing their delays and achieving timing optimization. Other techniques, like path-based techniques [7, 11, 19] directly model path timing constraints during placement, allowing for explicit optimization by manipulating cell coordinates. While the Elmore model is highly integrable into the placement process, it suffers from inaccuracy compared to sign-off tool, particularly as wirelength increases or the number of fanouts in a net grows. Typically, wire delays are more challenging to calculate than gate delays due to their black-box nature. As shown in Fig. 3, statistical analysis reveals that as the timing path length increases, the contribution of interconnection wire delays becomes increasingly significant. Consequently, inaccuracies in delay estimation, especially for wire delays, cannot be ignored. For example, in some cases, compared to sign-off wire delay, the Elmore delay error can exceed 800ps, as illustrated in Fig. 3. Such inaccuracies can lead to incorrect critical-path timing evaluations and negatively affect timing optimization during placement. In addition to the Elmore model, more accurate models have been proposed, such as the D2M (Delay with 2 Moments) delay metric [5], which accounts for the second moment of the impulse response, as well as other complex timing models like the Arnoldi model and the AWE (Asymptotic Waveform Evaluation) model, typically used at the post-routing stage. However, regardless of the method used, the issue of incomplete timing information before sign-off remains. This leads to discrepancies between the timing analysis conducted before sign-off and the final timing results after sign-off. To narrow the gap between timing analysis and the sign-off golden result, machine learning methods can be used for timing prediction. 3 Problem Formulation

In the problem formulation, besides timing accuracy evaluation, it is also crucial to incorporate timing considerations into the design flow. The term timing “slack” is commonly used to determine a critical timing path, which is defined as the difference of the required arrival time minus the arrival time. Negative slack means the path arrival time does not meet the requirement. The problem formulation of derivable timing model and integration for early timing optimization at placement stage can be described as follows: Problem. Given netlists and input layouts, cell library, and timing reports generated by the golden sign-off timer, the objective is to train a derivable delay predictor that minimizes the discrepancy between the predicted delay

5 Derivable Timing Prediction

In this work, the objective of our timing analysis is to improve the accuracy of the traditional timing model, while keeping its characteristics of convenient usage to combine with or even replace traditional model in prior timing optimization algorithms. The key techniques include the generation of the look-ahead RC network, the feature selection & extraction method, and the derivable ML algorithm.

5.2 Feature Extraction In timing prediction, feature selection is of critical importance to the effectiveness of model application. The look-ahead RC network is applied for timing feature extraction. Table 1 lists the features for our net-based delay model. Each net-based sample is a connection between a driver input pin and a target sink, i.e., b→din Fig. 5, the features can be divided into driver-related features and interconnection-related features. The driver-related features include: (1) Driving strength: It is extracted by the cell type, and it determines the output impedance of the driver. Typically, the higher the driving strength, the smaller the net delay. (2) Output load: It is the load capacitance of driver. (3) Fanout number: The number of sink pins of the net. (4) NLDM gate slew: It represents the speed of rise and fall transitions, and thus influences timing. (5) NLDM gate delay: It is the delay of the timing arc within the driving gate. It should be mentioned that, both gate slew and gate delay are not the golden result from the sign-off timer. Instead, they are obtained by the NLDM look-up table with two input indexes: the input slew and the output load of the driver, both of which are computed with the help of our look-ahead RC network. The interconnection-related features include: (1) Distance: It is the Manhattan distance between the locations of the driving output pin and the target sink pin. (2) Elmore delay: We select the capacity and resistance from the RC tree, and compute the Elmore delay between the driving output pin and the target sink pin. (3) Sink slew: The total slew of all sink pins, along with its associated impulse and capacitive load, is calculated by the Elmore delay model. It represents the quality of the signal transmission. Each two-pin net is then routed by L-shape routing. A look-ahead RC tree can be generated from the routing analysis and the cell library. The features of each two-pin net are then extracted. The extracted features of the two-pin net b→dare given in Fig. 5 as an example. It should be mentioned that, to consider rising and falling delays in the same predictor, for each two-pin net, different data samples are generated according to the combinations of rising and falling signals, i.e., at most 2 × 2 = 4 combinations. For each two-pin net, its rising and falling samples are varied by different related-features and timing labels. More timing features can further optimize timing prediction, but would require more feature computation and increase model complexity. To do the trade-off between timing performance and complexity, only the above timing features are selected in our placement.

5.3 Derivable GBM Model We choose the GBM model, a kind of decision tree-based algorithm, for its proven effectiveness with tabular and discrete data [17], making it well-suited for pin-to-pin and net-based timing predictions, i.e. XGBoost, LightGBM. However, common GBM methods are not derivable, which, to some extent, constrains their usability and adaptability in the context of timing optimization during the placement process. To address this limitation, the soft gradient boosting machine (sGBM) framework [16] is applied. This framework ingeniously connects multiple derivable base learners into a cohesive, derivable system. It achieves this by introducing the local losses and the global losses, drawing inspiration from the GBM concept. Our derivable basic learner makes use of the multilayer perceptron (MLP). MLP is a modern feedforward neural network composed of distinct layers, which includes at least an input layer, a hidden layer, and an output layer. At its core, MLP relies on three fundamental elements: weights, biases, and activation functions. The MLP model is essentially a function made up of multiple nonlinear and linear transformations. Importantly, each of these individual transformations is derivable, ensuring that the entire function expression is also derivable. Specifically, assuming that a MLP contains an input layer, a hidden layer, and an output layer, the MLP function can be expressed as Equation (2).

In this study, we adopt the net-weighting technique to demonstrate timing optimization using derivable timing model, as net-weighting technique is commonly employed in timing-driven placement and also applicable to other non-derivable model for performance comparison. In net-weighting technique, it assigns different weights to different nets such that the placer minimizes the weighted sum of net wirelength shown in Equation (5).

where x, y are cell coordinates, w(e) is the weight of net e, and the density constraint is usually transformed into a density penalty term with the density penalty weight λ. In an analytical placement, this optimization problem is solved by gradient descent on the objective function. Although the net-weighting technique appears to be straightforward, generating an effective net-weighting scheme is still not an easy task. Assigning bigger weights to critical nets typically reduces their wirelength and delays, but it can also lead to length growth and worsen timing of other near-critical nets, therefore the emergence of new critical path. To solve the problem, we introduce a dynamic net-weighting method, which involves updating weights based on the above derivable timing model during placement process. Many dynamic net-weighting techniques primarily set weights for the whole netse∈Eas shown in Equation (5). A key application of timing analysis is the identification of timing-critical paths with negative endpoint slacks. Path slack is defined as the difference between the required time and the arrival time at an endpoint, such as a flip-flop (FF) or a primary output. The slack value is crucial to identify violated paths that require further timing optimization. In our experiments, slacks greater than a threshold, i.e., -15ps in PrimeTime report, are classified as non-critical paths, while negative slacks below this threshold are classified as critical paths. This is because minor negative violations can be easily corrected through cell library retiming, and the margins provided by on-chip variation (OCV) and clock uncertainty settings during verification ensure that such small violations do not result in functional failures.

7.4 Discussion on Derivable Timing Model Recent studies [20, 38] have also explored derivable AI models such as GNNs and Transformers for timing prediction. To evaluate our approach with other derivable models, we give the prediction correlation analysis on pin-to-pin delay and path arrival time as shown in Table 8. For the GNN-based timing prediction model [20], a direct comparison is not possible since it requires a specific library format as input for GNN embedding and has not been released for the 28 nm technology node used in our experiments. To address this, we re-implemented the GNN model, preserving its key components: net embedding and delay propagation, while adapting it to our experimental environment. Our implementation employs the same feature set as “delayGBM”, trained with the Adam optimizer, mixed-precision training, and MSE loss function. As shown in Table 8, compared to “delayGBM”, which achieves a pin-to-pin delay correlation of 0.96, the correlation of the GNN model is 0.94. The average correlation of path arrival time is also lower than delayGBM, especially for larger circuits. In addition, circuit graph construction for the GNN model introduces significant complexity. The GNN inference time is about 20∼100x longer, averaging more than 90x longer than “delayGBM”. While the primary strength of GNNs lies in their ability to automatically learn graph features, this advantage may be less pronounced when highly correlated features are already explicitly provided, suggesting that the potential of GNNs in our context requires further investigation. Since the GNN model is significantly more complex than “delayGBM”, we believe that a more refined, GNN-specific embedding strategy is required during placement, which we leave for future work. 7.5 Discussion on Model Application In recent years, with the growing research on timing prediction models, increasing attention has been paid to their integration into design flows. During timing-driven placement, timing optimization methods can be built upon classical placement algorithms, where nets with timing violations receive special treatment. By adjusting net weights, or path constraints, timing convergence can be achieved through improved delay. This work proposes a derivable timing prediction model and use its derivablility to guide net weighting. Furthermore, for path constraints methods like [19], the derivable model can, in principle, serve as a substitute for Elmore delay in constructing constraint functions. In addition, prior works [4, 15] have explored the use of timing prediction models to guide commercial EDA tool optimization via timing annotation commands. Unlike the timing-driven optimizations discussed above during placement, these approaches allow for more aggressive and flexible design modifications, including buffer insertion, gate sizing, and logic restructuring. It should be noted that the effectiveness of integrating prediction models through path constraints or tool commands requires further investigation. In principle, the predictions from our model or other AI-based models could be used with tool commands to enable more accurate timing analysis, allowing the tool to apply more effective optimization strategies. Specifically, open questions include whether to adopt refined path-margin annotation ratios to adjust predicted slack [4], apply path grouping [15], and other related strategies. These directions will be studied in future work.
