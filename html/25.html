Machine learning application for cell delay accuracy improvement at post-placement stage: A case study for combinational cells

Yassine Attaoui

Abstract. In the early VLSI design stages, as most interest is given to timing closure, various techniques are deployed to satisfy the timing constraints and close design timing. Nevertheless, timing still needs to be accurate at the early stages due to the lack of physical design information. Therefore, the challenge for most EDA Logic Synthesis engines is to improve the overall timing accuracy at higher levels of abstraction. In this paper, we shall reduce the error gap in Cell Delay estimation for a placed gate-level netlist. We will generate the post-placed netlist from two different Siemens EDA tools. The purpose is to improve the accuracy of the cell delays of Oasys-RTL Synthesis tool, based on accurate cell delays from Aprisa PnR. We have trained and tested various Machine Learning models on several 16 nm designs. We achieved the highest accuracy using the Random Forest model, where the average accuracy is 91.25%. Furthermore, as the accuracy of the Cell delay is improved, the average Root-Mean-Square-Error (RMSE) is reduced from 16.958 to 4.469.

Nowadays, VLSI systems are increasingly complex, and the timing closure evolves as a challenge after each step of the development flow. The circuit’s timing closure at a high level of abstraction has always been the focus of all EDA companies after ensuring the circuit’s functionality. 2. Related work

Recently, Machine Learning applications have been increasingly growing in VLSI. Various EDA and IC design companies are exploring ML approaches that reduce the time-to-market and manufacturing runtime while assuring IC functionality. As a result, numerous applications are used at different design stages and using diverse approaches. This section studies various state-of-the-art ML applications related to VLSI Power, Performance, Area (PPA), and routing improvement. [4] presents a practical ML application for area prediction from a high-level specification of RTL components. The model achieved a high accuracy regardless of lacking netlist information at the specification level. From the HLS stage, [5] implemented a model to predict routing congestion. The routing bottlenecks were determined at early stages and resolved from the RTL level. In [6], the author tries to review power-grid-related issues such as IR drop and Electromigration (EM) using ML approaches. It implements a Machine Learning model that predicts the EM aging of the power grid network, as well as predicts a reliable power grid network by predicting the widths of metal lines. In terms of power analysis, [7] suggested an ML model that achieves high power estimation accuracy for RTL blocks across different benchmark designs. Compared to commercial gate-level power analysis tools, the author’s model reached satisfying accuracy and performance. In [8], the author suggested an ML model that improves the switching power accuracy. Various runs are conducted using (SPEF) file back-annotated RC parasitics. The trained model predicts the cell’s switching power

without needing a SPEF file spefless flow, which has achieved good accuracy and reduced error on switching power. In [9], the author proposes a predictive machine learning model for PBA (Path-Based timing Analysis) from GBA (Graph-Based timing Analysis). The model reduces the gap of PBA-GBA divergence consisting of arrival time and slack accuracy. Furthermore, since cell delay estimated using GBA-mode is more pessimistic than PBA-mode, the model arrives to reduce the PBA-GBA timing pessimism within a reduced runtime relative to ordinary PBA-mode. The model has achieved a well timing– runtime tradeoff with reduced predictive arrival time divergence and lower runtime. The author in [10] leverages the timing uncertainty at the prerouting stage by implementing the Random Forest algorithm. The model has largely reduced the pessimism on net delays with improved pre-routing net delay predictions. Our study aims to bridge the gap between the pre and post-placement stages by utilizing machine learning techniques. Our approach also extends the accuracy of the design into the early stages of the design process, precisely the Synthesis stage. Unlike [10], which utilizes small designs with a maximum of 1400 flip-flops, our approach addresses the real challenges and difficulties encountered in actual System-on-Chip (SoC) implementations. A similar approach is conducted in [11] to improve timing accuracy at the pre-route stage. The author proposed a GNN-based (Graph Neural Network) machine learning framework to correct the arrival time and slack at endpoints. The timing engine GNN-based model has reached accurate pre-routing net delay and pin arrival time within a short inference time compared to the complete routing and STA flow. The authors worked on small and medium-sized designs and predicted routing impact on timing to guide the placement at the physical implementation. However, the feature set used for prediction was kept minimal. [12] proposes an ML-based routing-free crosstalk prediction framework. The model classifies nets that may have large coupling capacitance or crosstalk noise. The authors predicted routing impact on timing to guide the placement at the physical implementation. In addition to improving timing at the routing stage, the authors also utilized small-sized designs, and few features were extracted. In [13], the author tries to minimize the divergence in path slack between an internal and a signoff STA tool. The model developed does not include cell delay miscorrelations. However, in [14], the author developed a learning-based approach to correlate the timing divergence for path slack, setup time, stage, cell, and wire delays. The experiment is conducted on 45 nm and 28 nm. The model corrects slack divergence that reaches a reduction of up to 7 times. This work introduces a machine learning approach to pre-place timing improvement of a synthesis tool. The delay predictions reach an accuracy near the post-place timing delay of a physical-aware P&R tool. We largely avoid pessimism by using ML to extract knowledge from a physical-aware timing engine. Our main idea is to decompose the path delay into a cell and net delay and focus on training the model to predict solely combinational cell delays. The predictions are timing-arc based, and the model predicts based on the cell unateness. We trained the model taking into account the timing_sense attribute. Our method significantly advances over previous works as it closes the gap between pre- and post-placement, taking accuracy further to the earlier Synthesis stage. As designs are getting more complex with billions of gates in a single SoC, the development and fabrication costs are increasing, and the PPA target is pushed to the limits. Improving the timing accuracy at upper development stages will help meet the PPA target without respine, reducing the development cost and time to market. Additionally, this work utilizes larger and real industrial technology node designs. Finally, while implementing various ML models, we extracted a large number of cell attributes around 101 features. As a result, the proposed model reaches considerable delay prediction accuracy on advanced technology nodes. The experimental results show that our approach can obtain a 91.25% delay accuracy and 4.469 Root-Mean-Squared-Error.

4.2. Model training

Regression model. Various state-of-art Regression models were studied. The chosen algorithms are Linear Regression, Decision Tree, Random Forest, Support Vector Regression, and K-Nearest Neighbors. The role of data in ML is crucial, and the quality of data is the key to improving the model’s accuracy. Consequently, we spent considerable time on the dataset processing. 4.1. Data processing

The shortlisted models were adapted and fitted to our datasets using Python Scikitlearn library [18]. In this chapter, we give a slight background of each model.

Multi-Linear Regression: The MLR is also known as multiple regression, which is an extension of single linear regression. This algorithm predicts continuous variables from multiple linear combinations of input variables. The algorithm defines a set of coefficients (weights) for each input variable. Then, it keeps correcting the coefficients based on the resulting accuracy of the predicted output variable [18,21].

Decision Tree: The DT is a non-linear algorithm that uses a tree model of decisions. DT is also used as regression to predict continuous data and is remarkably powerful that can fit complex datasets. The tree of decisions contains nodes and leaves, the nodes are tests on the attributes, and the leaves are the results of the tests [16,18,22].

In this chapter, we present different data pre-processing steps. We collected data by generating a collection of CSV files as in the flow in Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2. Then, the CSV files corresponding to cell and net properties, cell-net connections, library cell attributes, cell delays, and QoR metrics were merged into one master CSV using inner juncture on the cell full name, from pin and to pin columns. Where the full name, from pin and to pin are specified as data-frame indexes (ID) of the master CSV and identify each timing arc. During data preparation, we dropped several columns from the master CSV due to their weak informative contribution to the prediction. In the whole pre-processing step, the Scikit-learn Pandas library [16,17] is used to manage the master CSV file as a Dataframe that represents all valid design timing arcs. Table 3 summarizes the Dataframe’s information used for the training and testing datasets. Every row of the Dataframe represents a timing arc, and every column is an attribute of the corresponding cell.

Random Forest: Also known as the Random Decision Forests, the RF is one of the powerful algorithms for data prediction. The RF is constructed from multiple decision trees, and the resulting outputs of each decision tree will help the RF predict the output variable value. Using multiple trees makes RF a well-fitting algorithm for most kinds of datasets [18,22].

As Aprisa golden data divides timing arcs into positive and negative unate, referred to as cell delay rise and cell delay fall attributes. Consequently, we trained the ML models first to predict delay rise attribute, which refers to positive unate timing arcs. Then, we carried a similar approach to predict delay fall. This paper contains the result accomplished on cell delay rise prediction, while delay fall prediction follows a similar approach.

Support Vector Regression: The regression part of the Support Vector Machine (SVM) can also be used to predict continuous data. Its mission is to approximate a large number of data to a linear function. However, the difference from linear regression is that the SVR sets a threshold error allowance around the function line, which authorizes and considers points within the threshold margins [18].

K-Nearest Neighbors: The KNN is a non-parametric algorithm also used for regression. The KNN’s purpose is to find similarities between the input and output variables. Then it selects the K closest inputs to build the non-linear function. The KNN weakness is the runtime, and the algorithm becomes slower while dealing with a large dataset [18].

4.3. Model evaluation

As part of data processing, outlier removal is a preliminary phase in data preparation. Outliers are anomalies in the dataset caused by the tool’s miscalculations due to the lack of information or errors while manipulating data. We started detecting outliers by plotting a scattered plot and identifying cells with massive output capacitance and slew differences. The cut-off using Inter Quartile Ranger (IQR) approach at 6-sigma has defined the upper and lower bounds from the mean value. We discarded only cells located beyond and below 6-sigma to drop the fewer possible outliers and keep data integrity while achieving higher accuracy. In Table 2, we summarize the outliers percentage per design. In Fig. 2, we display a scattered plotting after outlier removal, the Xaxis is the Oasys cell delay, and Y-axis is the Aprisa cell delay. We also highlight the output capacitance as a heatmap. Finally, these outlier timing arcs were dumped from the TC1, TC2, TC3, and TC4 dataframes. However, TC5 is considered new raw data, and no outlier was removed. We remarked that these outliers are High Fanout cells and will be treated in a separate paper using clustering algorithms.

Once the model is fitted to the dataset and trained to predict new data, we evaluate the quality of predictions since the purpose is not only to build a model but to create a high-quality model with high predictive accuracy. We have chosen the Root-Mean-Squared-Error and Mean-Absolute-Error as metrics to measure the error rate of each model, and we used R2 score to evaluate how good the predictions are. We compared each model’s performance with these metrics and drew a conclusion in the next section [18].

In this paper, we studied a Machine Learning application for cell delay prediction for a post-placed gate-level netlist. Our purpose is to improve the accuracy of cell delays at an early design stage. We trained and tested five Machine Learning models with the help of several industrial designs. We have improved Oasys cell delay accuracy based on Aprisa Backend information, which we consider golden data. The proposed ML models achieved a higher prediction accuracy and lower error rate. The Random Forest model had the highest accuracy and lowest RMSE. Consequently, we reduced the average RMSE from 16.958 to 4.469 with an average accuracy of 91.25%. The Random Forest also produced higher stability and predictive capability for new design circuits. With more data added to train the Random Forest model, we can achieve an excellent generalization ability. The objective is to use this model integrated into a Python framework right after the logic synthesis stage. The model will provide precise estimations without the need for physical information. Based upon the selected features highlighted in the last chapter, the model will use only attributes within the Oasys synthesis tool. The model will support R&D and QA engineers to detect the tool’s irregularity and spotlight outliers engendered during logic synthesis. In terms of runtime, the Random Forest model delivers accurate values within a few seconds depending on design complexity, which perfectly matches our purpose. As a future work and extension to this study, we look forward to improving the training dataset with additional large designs and enhancing the overall model’s accuracy. We plan to investigate and implement other complex algorithms to enhance the model’s performance and integrate it into a comprehensive Python-based framework for wider application and accessibility.
