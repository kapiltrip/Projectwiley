Knowledge transferring framework for cell library characterization

Zhengguang Tang

Abstract. To evaluate digital circuit performance across PVT (process, voltage, and temperature) corners, standard cell library characterization requires costly simulations. Leveraging machine learning (ML) can improve the efficiency of this process. However, existing ML-based methods for cell library characterization often neglect the knowledge embedded across different timing arcs, leading to the need for extensive training data. In this paper, we propose a transfer learning (TL) framework to enhance timing characterization across multiple timing arcs. By quantifying the similarity among training tasks in the cell library using a fine-grained metric, our method enables rapid and accurate cell delay predictions through pre-training knowledge. Compared to conventional ML approaches, our TL framework improves both prediction accuracy and efficiency. Experimental results on 45 nm MOSFET and 14 nm FINFET technologies show significant error reductions of up to 80% and 67%, respectively.

In the modern integrated circuit designs, standard cell libraries serve as the fundamental building blocks for the entire design and optimization process [1,2]. To evaluate the performance of standard cell under different work conditions, library characterization is essential. Considering traditional characterization process involves the utilization of numerous SPICE simulations, there are two challenges need to be addressed. On the one hand, industry-standard cell libraries typically comprise hundreds to thousands of cell types [3], encompassing various logical functions and drive strengths. On the other hand, as technology nodes shrink to nanometer scales, PVT increasingly affect chip performance [4–6]. This results in a significant increase in PVT corners with technology scaling, especially exceeding 1000 when considering process variations [7]. Therefore, the combination of aforementioned characterization requirements will result in an exponential increase in simulation runtime.

In recent years, many ML-based methods are introduced to accelerate the generation of cell library. The underlying ML models include random forest (RF) [8], support vector regression (SVR) [9], feed-forward neural network (FFNN) [10], polynomial ridge regression (PRR) [11–15]. In particular, existing works [8–15] focus on training predictive models for individual timing arcs within library cells. 3. Proposed framework

The standard cell library comprises various cells with multiple logical functions and drive strengths. Due to variations in cell performance under different input conditions, each cell requires simulation for different timing arcs. For each timing arc, the results are represented using look-up tables (LUTs) across different indexes, serving as fundamental components in the standard cell library. The number of components is typically in the thousands [12].

The overview of our proposed framework is illustrated in Fig. 3. As shown in this figure, three main steps are defined. In the first step, simulation samples are generated to create training and testing datasets (see Section 3.1). Next, baseline models are trained using an ensemble bagging strategy (see Section 3.2). Finally, based on task grouping methods, we apply feature-based TL algorithm to explore embedded knowledge and enhance accuracy for baseline models (see Section 3.3).

3.1. Data domains sampling

When using ML methods to characterize the standard cell library, modeling each basic component with LUT-formed data can be regarded as an individual learning task. As shown in Fig. 1, the traditional approaches involve generating LUT data for various timing arcs to create simulation datasets. Each simulation dataset is then treated as an independent learning task for fitting individual models [8–15]. To achieve accurate predictions, traditional methods rely on increasing the number of simulations to expand the training dataset.

The implementation for domains sampling are presented in the left part of Fig. 3. Considering that similar timing behaviors exist across different timing arcs, in this work we chose the open-source Nangate 45 nm [19] and Nangate 14 nm [20] cell libraries with multiple sets of device models to characterize cell delays. In addition, all parameters for device and corner are provided by predictive technology model (PTM) [21].

Unlike the aforementioned works, we use TL methods to explore embedded knowledge among similar learning tasks, aiming to improve accuracy and efficiency without incurring additional simulation costs. Furthermore, our proposed TL methods is scalable to multiple underling regression models, and the effectiveness will be demonstrated in Section 4.

Traditional ML techniques heavily depend on available training data, where the quality and quantity play critical roles in the training process. Limited or inaccurate training data can significantly degrade prediction performance. Generating missing data directly for the target task is challenging, but leveraging knowledge from a related domain can be valuable [16]. TL offers an effective approach to transferring knowledge between different domains or tasks, particularly beneficial for improving ML model performance when labeled data is costly [17]. Typically, the task and sample domain of interest are referred to as the target task and domain, while similar tasks and sample domains are known as source tasks and domains.

Each timing arc in the cell library need to be characterized under every PVT corner. The features (corner-related parameters) and labels (LUT-formed cell delays) are stored in specific domains. Given a cell library for technology tcontaining Mtiming arcs, we denote the domain corresponding to the mth timing arc rmas D(t)rm. For training the following baseline models and performing TL optimization, the features and labels (illustrated in model training part of Fig. 1) from these domains will serve as the inputs and ground truth for prediction models.

3.2.2. Underlying ML model implementation

We define the underlying model as the type of ML model used for the sub-models. The learning task for cell delay prediction is a typical regression task. And different types of underlying ML models including RF [8], SVR [9], FFNN [10], and PRR [11–15] are employed to solve this regression task. Based on our experiments, configuring the optimal structure and fitting the extensive model parameters for each task using the FFNN model requires significant runtime. Furthermore, the predictive accuracy of the FFNN model is not optimal (more details can be found in Section 4.2). Therefore, three other ML models (RF, SVR, and PRR) are chosen as underlying models to demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed framework.

The adopted ensemble bagging learning strategy involves training multiple instances of the same underlying model on different subsets of the training data, employing a random selection with replacement (known as bootstrapping). In addition, the sampling ratio is set to 0.8, indicating that each subset comprises 80% of samples from the training data. Finally, the baseline regression prediction is obtained by averaging the predictions of these sub-models. This approach enables the improvement of the overall performance of the model by reducing overfitting and increasing robustness. As illustrated in the step 2 in Fig. 3, balancing efficiency and accuracy considerations, 3 sub-models are separately trained for each learning task.

Furthermore, to reduce the needed training models for cell library characterization, we use multi-output modeling to predict multiple cell delays under the same input conditions in a non-linear delay model (NLDM) formatted cell library. 4. Experiment and evaluation results

4.1. Experimental setup

The previous work [15] introduces a similarity evaluation method based on edit distance and Hamming distance. It considers that the smaller the difference among Boolean functions of timing arcs, the more similar are the learning tasks of timing arcs. However, this is an intuition-based and coarse-grained metric. For the demand of cell library characterization with a large number of different learning tasks, a theoretical and fine-grained similarity evaluation metric is required.

To address this issue, we utilize the Wasserstein distance [22] to evaluate the similarity among different learning tasks. The Wasserstein distance can be computed as:

W(P, Q) = inf γ∈Π(P,Q) E(x,y)∼γ[‖x−y‖] , (2)

To validate the effectiveness of our proposed framework, we conduct experiments on 14 nm FINFET and 45 nm MOSFET cell libraries [19,20] across various PVT corners listed in Table 1. The tested cells constitute a shared subset of library cells with identical logical functions and drive strengths from both the Nangate 14 nm and Nangate 45 nm libraries. As summarized in Table 3, 48 cells are chosen for testing in each technology. According to varying input conditions across different cells in each technology, we need to model a total of 213 timing arcs to predict 799 cell delay types. In other words, our framework will be executed once for every timing arc. To prepare training data for each timing arc, we simulate cell delays based on a 7 × 7 index dimension. During the training process for each timing arc, 30% of the samples from the task-specific sample pool are randomly selected as the test set, while the remaining 70% constitute the complete training set. For testing with different training sizes, additional samples are randomly selected from the full training set and combined with the initial 100 samples used for distance computation to create the actual available training set. Moreover, the available training sets selected in the source domain and the target domain through the aforementioned process serve as the inputs DSand DTfor Algorithm 1, respectively.

where Π(P, Q) represents the set of all possible joint distributions γ formed by combining the distributions Pand Q, xand yrepresent samples from the distributions Pand Q, respectively, while ‖x−y‖ denotes the Euclidean distance between these samples. The Wasserstein distance measures the minimum cost of transforming one probability distribution in the domain Pinto another in the domain Q. For cell library characterization, since we expect similar tasks to exhibit similar trends or characteristics for the same input features, we first randomly select 100 combinations from the complete feature space. These selected feature combinations are then used to locate the corresponding samples in each task-specific sample pool to compute the Wasserstein distance. We believe that if two different tasks exhibit smaller Wasserstein distances for the sample labels under the same feature combinations, their corresponding behaviors are more similar. This indicates that better transfer learning performance can be achieved. In addition, to address the variability in delay scales across different cells, the delay values of the selected samples are first normalized.

4.2.4. Efficiency comparisons

By leveraging an efficient TL strategy, we can reduce the number of required simulations and overall learning runtime needed to achieve a specific accuracy goal. Experimenting with PRR for optimal performance, Fig. 9 shows the trends in the amount of training samples and RMSEs for the baseline model and the TL model. To achieve the best accuracy of the baseline model, our TL strategy reduces the required simulation costs by 1.96× and 1.75× for the 45 nm and 14 nm libraries, respectively.

In addition, we conduct an ablation study on our proposed bagging ensemble and TL strategies. As presented in Table 4, when using complete training set and testing on the baseline model with PRR, both the bagging and TL techniques independently contribute to performance improvements. Notably, the combination of all techniques yields the most substantial enhancement, achieving a remarkable RMSE reduction of 82% and 69% for the 45 nm and 14 nm libraries, respectively. Furthermore, by averaging the results of timing arcs within cells sharing the same logic function (disregarding differences in input number and drive strength) and subsequently averaging across all 213 tested timing arcs, we observe that the performance gain achieved through the TL strategy is significantly more pronounced compared to that achieved through the bagging strategy. This indicates that the TL method plays a crucial role in enhancing model prediction performance.

Moreover, based on the best RMSEs achieved by the PRR model on the largest training set, as shown in Fig. 9, we compare the total runtime of the PRR model with that of our proposed TL model. These results are summarized in Table 5. For the PRR model, the training runtime includes data processing/loading, model training, and validation. The training runtime of the TL model additionally accounts for the similarity evaluation and TL process. However, the variation in training runtime is negligible compared to the training data preparation time required for SPICE simulations. Using the trained model for inference, our approach significantly reduces the runtime for generating all 48 libraries listed in Table 1, achieving a 63% reduction for the Nangate 45 nm library and 60% for the Nangate 14 nm library.
