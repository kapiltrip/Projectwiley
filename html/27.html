Early RTL delay prediction using neural networks

Daniela Sánchez Lopera

Abstract. Nowadays, the digital chip design flow starts with formal specifications, which are mapped to Register Transfer Level (RTL) models using different underlying (micro-) architectures. By doing so, a hardware designer predicts and resolves time-critical parts to achieve an RTL-design that meets all constraints after synthesis. However, wrong predictions can be detected only later in the design flow, thus leading to long design iterations. Classical methods estimating delay in early design stages are constrained to the type of components or are computationally expensive for larger designs. This paper proposes a machine learningbased approach to estimate pin-to-pin delays for RTL combinational circuits. To improve the quality of the predictions we combine slew and delay estimation. To that end, training data are built using features of components generated by a model-driven hardware generator framework. Ground truth labels for delays, slews, and their interdependencies are extracted using open-source tools for logic synthesis and static timing analysis. Two different datasets are built: one targeting logic gates and multiplexers and an enlarged one, which generalizes to more RTL primitives A model trained using the former dataset achieves, on average, a coefficient of determination R2 of 87% when evaluating over 4-bit prefix adders. Using the enlarged dataset, the best model reaches an R2 of 77%. On average, our models are 8.4× faster w.r.t the time required to run synthesis and timing analysis. Results show that generalizing to more primitives decreases the models’ performance, but the runtime benefit is maintained. Based on the delay estimation, critical areas of the design can be detected and proper microarchitecture decisions can be taken earlier and faster in the design flow.

1. Introduction

Early estimations of the delay information of a design at RTL can ease the design-space exploration to find the optimal implementation and architectural option. Smarter decisions in earlier design stages result in fewer iterations of the complete EDA flow, higher Quality of Results (QoRs), and potentially, less time-to-market. In particular, Hardware (HW) generator frameworks can benefit from earlier predictions, which could be used to evaluate implementation decisions during the generation without any human-in-the-loop. Classical methods estimating delay from a higher level of abstraction use polynomial, macromodeling, and analytical approaches. They have several drawbacks: they are slow, constrained by the type of components, computationally expensive, or give only a very rough estimation. In the last decade, ML has proven its ability to support classification and prediction tasks in many fields with higher accuracy than classical methods. Consequently, it is being incorporated into the EDA flow, especially in logic synthesis, placement, and verification [2]. This paper proposes Neural Networks (NNs) for pin-to-pin component delay estimation of RTL components in Application-Specific

The chip design flow is a complex and iterative process. First, informal specifications are mapped to system-level descriptions. Those are further translated into functional, logical, and physical designs using Electronic Design Automation (EDA) tools. Hereby, synthesis tools optimize the gate-level netlist to meet timing constraints while minimizing area and power consumption [1]. Before fabrication and packaging, the correct functionality of the design has to be verified, and the compliance of design metrics in terms of Power, Performance, and Area (PPA) has to be validated. Principally, designs must meet timing constraints and work at the desired clock frequency. Although transformation and optimization have been automated using EDA tools, it is the task of the hardware designer to map initial specifications to Register Transfer Level (RTL) models that intentionally meet the timing requirements. This mapping is challenging, as RTL models can be built using multiple different implementations or (micro-) architectures, whose timing behaviors are known only after costly synthesis and timing analysis steps.

TA is an EDA tool to determine whether a design works properly at the required clock period. TA can be dynamic or static. The dynamic analysis simulates the complete circuit using input vectors and evaluating the outputs. STA is a static approach, as it propagates the gate delays from primary inputs to outputs. The gate delay is the time needed for a signal to propagate through a gate, i.e., going from input to output port [4].

In STA, all paths in a design are analyzed using gate delays and signal arrival times, especially the path with maximum and minimum delay. STA provides a severity measure called slack: a negative slack means that the signal through the path is too late, a positive slack means that the cells in the path could be replaced by ones with higher delay (and potentially, lower area) without causing timing violations [5].

In this paper, the training data are built using a model-driven HW generator framework. Targeting a specific CMOS ASIC technology, component features and ground truth labels are extracted using opensource EDA tools. Fig. 1 shows the complete data collection flow. ML models using one-hot encoded and embedding feature vectors are trained and fine-tuned. The trained models are used to get estimated pin-to-pin delay values. To improve the performance of our models, slew information is included in the feature set. Moreover, we sequentially predict slew and delay values per component and then propagate the predicted slew along the delay path.

To evaluate the models’ performance, hardware adders are used as proof of concept. Specifically, three different architectures of 4-bit prefix adders with three different input transition times are compared. The trained models were used to predict all the pin-to-pin delays of all primitive components in the designs. When training a model with a dataset of logic gates and multiplexers, a simple Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) using one-hot encoding achieves, on average, a coefficient of determination R2 of 87% w.r.t delay values from the EDA tool. The MLP model using embedding feature vectors achieves an R2 score of 79%. Training with an enlarged dataset, the best results are obtained by the MLP architecture using an embedding input layer. This approach reaches, on average, an R2 of 77% and a Mean Squared Error (MSE) of 0.68%. Both models are around 8.4× faster than the EDA tools.

STA highly depends on the delay values used for each gate in the whole design. STA tools obtain the delay values using a Non-linear Delay Model (NLDM). Specifically, the delay of a cell is defined as a Lookup Table (LUT) with two variables as indexes: input slew and fanout load. The former is the time that a signal requires to make a transition. If the signal changes from low to high, it is called a rising edge. From high to low, it is called a falling edge. The fanout is the capacitive load at the output [4]. For a constant output load, an increase in the input slew will reduce the delay. For a constant input transition, the delay will increase when the fanout capacitance increases. The slew is propagated along the circuit. The output slew of a cell is the input slew of the next connected cell in the timing path. Output slews are also defined via LUTs with input slew and fanout load as indexes. An NLDM stores delay and slew LUTs in a technology library as the Synopsis Liberty file [6]. STA tools can read those liberty files. During TA, the tool uses interpolation or extrapolation to calculate delays and output slews for index values that are not specified or outside the defined index tables. Moreover, STA tools are also able to scale the delay and slew values according to the current operating conditions, which physically affect the cell behavior such as temperature, voltage, and process [1].

The results show that generalizing to more component types decreases the R2 score. Nevertheless, RTL designs are not solely composed of logic gates and multiplexers. Thus, using the enlarged dataset is considered the most viable approach. Compared to EDA tools, our approach represents a good trade-off between the quality of the predictions and runtime.

In this paper, an NLDM is employed for extracting ground truth labels for the delay and slew values. The prediction of these metrics is defined as a multi-objective regression problem that can be solved using ML.

2.2.3. Model selection

Once the training data and their features are selected, the architecture of the ML model is defined so that the last layer outputs the predicted vector. In this paper, an MLP [13] is used due to its simplicity and good performance in multi-objective tasks. A simple MLP can represent a wide variety of functions when using appropriate parameters. Fig. 2(a) sketches the main layers of an MLP: input, hidden, and output layer. The input layer is made of as many neurons as the number of input features. For all our models, the number of hidden layers, as well as the number of nodes for those, are hyperparameters. The output layer has as many neurons as the number of objectives. The modules are fully connected, i.e., the value of each element of the subsequent layer is the value of the so-called activation function applied to the weighted sum of all the values of the current layer. The NN uses the backpropagation learning method to update all weights during training.

Hyperparameters are model parameters that cannot be trained. Using optimal hyperparameters improves the model performance. Thus, hyperparameter search should be conducted. Manual tuning, grid search, and random search are considered brute-force methods. Grid and random search perform better than manual tuning, but they do not consider past evaluations. Hence, they lose significant time by evaluating hyperparameters in the same inadequate region [17]. Therefore, a model-based sequential method called Tree-Structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) was introduced [17]. TPE creates a surrogate model of the objective function and considers past evaluations to choose new hyperparameters. This work uses TPE to fine-tune all models.

3. Related work

2.2.2.4. Shapley values: Shapley values [11] are a concept from game theory that is being used for explainable and interpretable Artificial Intelligence (AI) [12]. Shapley regression values measure feature importance for linear models, i.e., compute which features contribute more to a prediction. To that end, two different models are trained with and without a chosen feature, respectively. The performance of both models is compared, and the differences are computed for all possible feature subsets. The final Shapley values are the weighted average over all possible subsets [12].

The Logical Effort (LE) method [18] relates the delay of a CMOS circuit to the LE value, which is defined as the ratio of the input capacitance of a gate, and the input capacitance of an inverter delivering the same output current. It is estimated as counting transistor widths for the gate divided by transistor widths for the inverter. The gate delay is defined as the sum of the effort delay and parasitic or zeroload delay. The effort delay is the LE multiplied by the fanout. The LE method allows comparing different implementations without running synthesis, it is technology-independent, and it can be used in multistage designs. However, it does not consider the effect of transition times of the inputs, and it uses estimations for the parasitic delay and LE values.

In [19], four analytical novel methods are introduced to estimate delay given the placement of a circuit. Using the same technology and same router, base model designs are used to extract factors such as net degree, route length, net delay, and route Steiner tree length. Those factors are terms of the estimation functions, which can be quadratic or linear equations. For instance, the proposed Delay Sampling method computes delay as a quadratic function of the pin-to-pin placement length. With base designs, the coefficients are computed, and for new designs, the equation is used with the current wire’s length. This method uses scaling factors and LUTs to link the estimated variables per net. Thus, it can be slow and computationally expensive for larger designs.

In [25], a one-shot scheme predicts the area-delay curve for Boolean functions. This method proposes a novel delay complexity measure based on the on and off-sets of a Boolean function. According to the authors, the size and the number of prime implicants of a function affect the time needed for evaluating the function. An STA tool is used to generate a family of curves of delay values versus the novel complexity measure. This method provides a minimum and maximum delay estimation. However, it does not support circuits with XOR and other complex gates.

3.0.4. Machine learning-based

In [20], the delay is estimated as a logarithmic function of the complexity of the gate and the fanout. Type of gate and fanout values are extracted from the designs before logic synthesis. These are scaled by constant coefficients or technology-dependent factors calculated using the NOT and NAND delay, the fanout limit, and the standard load. In this paper, the authors did not consider the effects of the signal transition times on the estimation.

In [26], an ML-based delay estimator is used during FPGA placement. They use ML for three main reasons. First, training, validation, and inference are faster than running traditional EDA tools. Delays are non-linear functions in which ML has proved high performance. Finally, an ML-based estimator can also be easily updated. They build a dataset with 20 features, where the most relevant are: number of pins, Half Perimeter Wire Length (HPWL), and average congestion.

In [21], the delay is approximated as a linear function of the number of programmable interconnect points and the number of segments between two circuit points. Scaling coefficients were computed from observations done using the Xilinx timing analysis tool. The approximation function estimates delay for Field Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGAs) considering also the wiring effects, but it does not support ASIC.

3.0.2. Macromodeling

Macromodeling is a usual way to estimate design metrics from a high-level circuit description. It can be seen as a template, in which the quantity under analysis is related to a set of parameters and coefficients. The set of parameters is computed by using a set of base designs. Commonly, linear regression is used to define the actual equation of the model.

Our paper is comparable to [26], as we defined the delay estimation as a regression problem. However, we estimate the delay at RTL and use delay training data after logic synthesis rather than after placement to avoid learning inefficient mappings done during physical synthesis. [26] targets FPGA, not ASIC technology, therefore they did not consider any effect of slew. [26] uses Random Forest (RF), and we use NNs, a more expensive method that can generalize better than RF when using appropriate parameters. To further improve, we perform a hyperparameter search. Compared to other methods, our approach can be used for all types of logic gates and more complex components. Moreover, the modeling part is easier, as only the dataset has to be generated rather than modeling the delay gate behavior with statistical or mathematical functions.

In [22], a macromodeling technique is used to estimate the delay at the behavioral level. Technology-independent gate-level netlists are mapped to Binary Decision Diagrams (BDDs). For delay estimation, the model relates various types of the complexity of a Boolean function to its gate-level implementation as size, number of inputs, and density.

4. Method

In [23], a macromodel for delay estimation at RTL is proposed as a function of Vddand Vt. The two scaling coefficients are extracted from experiments in HSPICE. However, the model only supports NAND, NOR, and NOT gates.

3.0.3. Polynomial-based

Polynomial models are regression-based, where the delay is the dependent variable, and design parameters are the independent variables.

Gate delay estimation is challenging due to the many dependencies: technology, operating conditions, cell types, outputs capacitance, and signal transition times. Therefore, we proposed a new approach to estimate cell delays using NNs. We define delay pin-to-pin estimation as a multi-objective regression problem. To get a more accurate delay estimation, the slew per gate is also predicted and propagated. The input for the regression model is a feature vector viwhere iis a pin-topin connection of a component within a design. The vector vencodes information of the features from Table 1. The output of the model yi is a vector of four components representing the regression objectives: [slew_out_fall, slew_out_rise, delay_fall, delay_rise]

4.2. Feature selection

For both datasets, the generated Verilog code is synthesized using Yosys,1 [29] an open-source tool that reads Verilog Hardware Description Language (HDL) files and creates a gate-level netlist by mapping it to CMOS cells of the technology library. We use a 40 nm technology for our experiments. The synthesized netlists are the required input to the STA. In this work, OpenSTA2 [30] is used, an open-source version of the commercial Parallax timer tool. A technology library configuration is chosen, and the tool is set up with parameters exemplarily shown in Table 3.

In this paper, different features are selected from two different datasets. We generate random configurations of components to build two different training sets: A simple dataset (DS-I) and a generalized dataset (DS-II).

4.2.1. DS-I

To consider variations of the slew, we generate timing reports with two and four different variants for the transition time of the input ports for the datasets DS-I and DS-II, respectively. The timing report style is configured using TCL scripts. Pin-to-pin timing reports are collected for all components within a design. To that end, the tool is configured to analyze all the four possible timing paths i.e. Registerto-Register, Register-to-Output, Register-to-Input and Input-to-Output, and the maximum edges: max rise and max fall. Automatically, we read the generated reports and extract information related to arc type, slew, and delay. These are used to find the maximum rise and fall delay and the corresponding slew values.

In this dataset, twenty types of RTL components are used to build the training designs: multiplexers, inverters, and logic gates (AND, NAND, OR, NOR, XOR, and XNOR). The latter in a bitwise, logical, and reduced fashion. In DS-I, each design contains only the abovementioned RTL components, with a randomly generated number of inputs and bit widths. The number of inputs varies from one up to 12. Input bit widths range from one to 32. To consider different fan outs, the number of components per design ranges from zero up to six. Single-component designs have zero connections to the output pin. Additionally, two different transition times of primary input ports are considered during the TA. Our collected dataset DS-I consists of 42.9K samples.

RTL features and information from EDA tools are mapped to a tabular dataset. Each row of the dataset or sample represents a pin-topin connection of an RTL component inside a circuit. Table 2 shows the number of samples per component category. The columns of the dataset contain the RTL features as well as ground truth labels. The generation of our dataset following the steps shown in Fig. 1 took around three weeks when running in a CPU machine.

5. Application

In the following, the performance of the models MLP and TabMLP is evaluated when using DS-I as the training dataset. In particular, this experiment analyzes the effect of learning slew values simultaneously and propagating them to the next connected component in the timing path.

Table 6 compares our estimated delay values w.r.t the results from the STA tool, when using the MLP and the TabMLP architectures. Table 6 shows the R2 value for fall and rise delay estimation. The MLP without slew information provides a low estimation accuracy. To increase the accuracy, we add slew features to the dataset. We incorporate slew estimation and propagation in our final method, as the input slew is known only for the input ports. The second last row of Table 6 confirms the strategy and shows the high R2 values. The same experiments were conducted using TabMLP.

Adders are a key component in ASIC designs. The simplest version of an adder is the Ripple Carry Adder (RCA). An N-bit RCA is built using Nfull adders, the carry-out bit of the previous stage becomes the carry-in of the current stage. At any particular time, only one full adder instance is used [34]. More complex architectural versions are the Parallel Prefix adders, as they split the computation into three stages: Pre-processing, Carry-propagation, and Post-processing [35]. Three main architectures of parallel prefix adders are shown in Fig. 7: SKA, BKA, and KSA. In the gray-colored stage, rectangles calculate the propagate and generate bits for primary inputs. In the blue stage, generate and propagate values per bit are calculated using: generate blocks (circles), generate and propagate blocks (dark circles), and buffers (triangles). In the final stage, total sum and carry bits are computed [35]. In general, KSA occupies more area and is faster than a BKA. SKA has the same delay as KSA, but it occupies a lesser area for a higher number of bits.

To evaluate the stability of the models across different input slew configurations, our models are run for both known configuration variants (V1 and V2) and one unknown but similar (V3). Table 7 shows that for V1 and V2, the results have a R2 score higher than 80% for all adders and both models. For V3, the R2 decreases as unknown input slews give a wrong predicted output slew, which will be propagated and affects the overall prediction of the delay. However, the MAE and MSE are still low. For simplicity, the results for rising and falling edges are averaged.

We implement the three architectures for 4-bit with one carry input and carry output bit. The three implementations have the same number of primary ports and bit widths. They also use three types of primitives: Bitwise XOR, AND, and OR. The maximum number of inputs per gate is two. Table 5 compares other details of the RTL implementation.

The MLP model achieves on average 6.76% MAE, 0.96% MSE, and R2 equal to 0.87. The experiments were repeated using the TabMLP model. Table 7 shows that this model achieves on average 9.49% MAE, 1.39% MSE, and R2 equal to 0.79.
