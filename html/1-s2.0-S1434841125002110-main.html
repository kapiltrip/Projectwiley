TimeBoost: Accelerating timing analysis engines using XGBoost

Anastasis Vagenas

Abstract. Timing analysis is an essential verification method for the design and optimization of integrated circuits, while it also constitutes the cornerstone of the final signoff. However, the increasing complexity of modern designs, driven by aggressive technology scaling, necessitates more efficient timing engines. In this article, we introduce TimeBoost, a novel learning-based approach for performance optimization of gate-level timing analysis. TimeBoost employs GPU-accelerated XGBoost classifiers to evaluate stage complexity and select the optimal timing analysis method, exploiting the available runtime and accuracy trade-off. Unlike prior works that predict timing directly, our approach ensures reliable and accurate timing estimation by leveraging timing analysis methods as prediction targets. Moreover, our models utilize representative electrical, topological, and gate features for each stage, which can be extracted with minimal effort. We assess several Machine Learning (ML) models, including XGBoost, Artificial Neural Networks, and K-Nearest Neighbors, and explore their different hyperparameters. Experimental evaluation on one million stages demonstrates that our MLbased approach achieves a runtime improvement of up to 3.86× over our best available method, imposing only a 3%–4% overhead to timing analysis while maintaining high accuracy. Thus, the proposed models can be integrated into signoff tools or timing-driven iterative optimization flows to expedite timing closure.

1. Introduction

Industrial gate models, such as the Non-Linear Delay Model (NLDM) and the more detailed Composite Current Source (CCS) model [7], are utilized to compute the driver output waveform and estimate gate timing. However, these models require the approximation of the distributed RC interconnect as a lumped capacitive load. The simplest load model is the sum of all interconnect capacitance values (Ctotal), while more accurate approaches involve the calculation of an effective capacitance (Ceff) [3,4,8,9] at the cost of additional runtime and memory overhead. On the other hand, the golden standard for interconnect analysis is SPICE simulation [10,11], but it is prohibitive due to its computational demands. To address this challenge, Model- Order Reduction (MOR) techniques [12–14] are commonly employed to create a Reduced-Order Model (ROM) of the interconnect, enabling more efficient simulation compared to using the original RC circuit. The most well-known methods, AWE and RICE [13,14], match the first few moments of the interconnect transfer function and create a ROM to approximate its transient behavior. Although both techniques can be highly accurate, they require significant computations and are prone to numerical instability, especially when using higherorder approximations. Timing analysis is a critical and demanding process in the design and verification of Very Large Scale Integrated (VLSI) circuits, which ensures that design timing constraints (e.g., setup and hold) are met. It plays a key role in iterative optimization flows, where a timing engine is repeatedly invoked at each step to verify and maintain timing integrity, while it also serves as the cornerstone of the final signoff that determines whether a chip is ready for fabrication [1]. As VLSI designs grow in complexity, incorporating billions of transistors and employing hundreds of analysis scenarios, timing analysis has emerged as the major bottleneck in achieving design closure, accounting for up to 60% of the total design time [2]. Moreover, as technologies scale below 7 nm, increased wire resistivity, signal non-linearities, the Miller effect, and strong gate-interconnect interdependencies render accurate timing analysis even more challenging [3–5]. Therefore, the efficiency and accuracy of a timing engine are crucial to achieving timing closure within reasonable turnaround times. In practice, both static [1] and dynamic [6] timing analysis is typically performed at the stage level, where each stage is composed of a driver gate, a distributed RC interconnect, and a single or multiple receiver gates. Stage timing analysis is further divided into two distinct but interdependent parts, namely gate and interconnect analysis.

2. Background

Fig. 1 illustrates a gate-level stage composed of a driver gate, a distributed RC interconnect, and multiple receiver gates. Each node i of the RC-tree interconnect has a capacitance to ground Ci, a downstream capacitance Ctoti (i.e., the sum of all the capacitances of the subtree starting at node i), a slew value Si, and is connected to each fanout node j via a resistance Ri→j. The sum of all interconnect Ri→j values is denoted as Rtotal.

During stage timing estimation, gate analysis calculates the driver’s output voltage waveform to determine the driver delay and slew, while interconnect analysis estimates the target receiver’s slew and interconnect delay. Together, these values yield the overall stage delay (i.e., the sum of the driver and interconnect delay) and slew (i.e., target receiver slew). Then, the computed stage delay is utilized to estimate the overall delay across various timing paths in the circuit, while the stage slew is used as input to the next gate-level stage [1].

Library-compatible gate models, such as NLDM and CCS [3], capture the timing and input capacitance information of each gate into Look-Up Tables (LUTs) indexed by input slew and output load. These precharacterized LUTs are used by gate-level timing analysis engines for accurate stage delay and slew estimation. A brief overview of the industrial NLDM and CCS models is provided below.

Recent efforts to accelerate timing analysis harness parallel computing, such as CPU multi-threading, GPUs, and CPU–GPU heterogeneous computing, exploiting the available parallelism within timing analysis tasks [19]. For instance, enhancing the efficiency of Multi-Corner Multi- Mode (MMCM) analysis [20], Path-Based Analysis (PBA) [21], or delay calculation [20–22] can lead to speedups of up to three orders of magnitude. However, such approaches are closely tied to the underlying timing analysis method, requiring significant modifications to the engine’s data structures and algorithms. Another mainstream approach for improving accuracy and efficiency is Machine Learning (ML) [23, 24], which has been extensively employed in characterization [25,26], modeling of complex effects [27,28], and timing estimation [4,29–32]. More specifically, the availability of scalable frameworks [33–35] and the ability of learning-based models to represent multi-dimensional data and complex relationships is particularly valuable for interconnect [29–31] and stage [4,32] timing estimation. The initial approach in [29] attempts to reduce the number of expensive signoff tool invocations by utilizing a linear regression model with several moment-based metrics as inputs. The authors in [30] used Random Forest regressors to estimate pre-routing timing and explored different features. In [31], Graph-Neural Networks (GNN) were used to accurately predict interconnect delay during signoff, considering interconnect topology and RC characteristics. Nevertheless, these works [29–31] focus only on interconnect timing, assuming accurate gate timing, and require the extraction and computation of multiple features before inference, such as moment-based metrics [29], driver and receiver locations [30], or interconnect topology and RC values [31]. To sidestep these limitations, [4, 32] focus on predicting stage timing, considering gate-interconnect interdependencies through their input features. The winners of the ACM TAU 2021 contest [4,36] leverage an efficient algorithm to extract stage features and provide initial stage timing estimates, and then employ ANNs to correlate with golden SPICE results. Similarly, [32] proposes a custom ANN model, which integrates Transformers and Residual Networks (ResNet), to estimate stage delay using multiple momentbased and Ceff-based features. A critical issue of most ML-based timing analysis methods is that they make direct predictions [4,29–32], which might be unreliable when the features deviate from the model’s training data space. Furthermore, they require large and diverse datasets that are often difficult to obtain, while the training process may demand considerable computational resources. Thus, although ML is a powerful tool for timing prediction, ensuring reliability is a persistent challenge. Finally, the authors in [37] utilized two filtering techniques to choose among different gate and interconnect algorithms. However, these techniques are applied independently, disregarding gate-interconnect interdependencies, while they assume pi-model loads and rely on heuristic moment-based thresholds.

2.2. Interconnect timing

During standard cell characterization, a lumped capacitive load is typically assumed [39,40]. Thus, a single capacitance value must be used to represent the highly resistive RC interconnect of each stage and access the LUTs. The simplest and most straightforward approximation of an RC interconnect load is Ctotal, which ignores the resistive shielding effect of complex on-chip interconnects, resulting in increased pessimism during gate timing analysis [3].

SPICE simulation [10,11] is widely regarded as the golden standard for interconnect timing estimation, offering the most accurate and reliable results. However, its computational requirements, particularly in terms of runtime and memory, render it impractical for interconnect timing analysis. In contrast, several analytical moment-based models, such as the Elmore delay and slew, D2M, and S2M, have been proposed for fast timing analysis of interconnects [15,16]. These are based on the first few moments of the RC interconnect impulse response, which, however, assume step inputs and ignore the resistive shielding effect. 3.2. Model evaluation

To assess the effectiveness of our feature selection and modeling, we investigated three types of ML models, namely, XGBoost (XGB), Artificial Neural Networks (ANN), and K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) classifiers. For each model type, we varied different hyperparameters and accuracy thresholds E (2%–8% RE against SPICE), generating multiple models. Specifically, the XGB models were trained using 40–180

estimators (N), ANNs employed three distinct architectures, while KNNs utilized three different algorithms. The ANNs were trained using the Adam optimizer [41], a maximum number of 2000 epochs, and the Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) activation function for hidden layers and softmax for the output. Note that in this article, the reported architecture refers to the hidden layers used, as the input and output layers depend on the number of input features and output classes, respectively. For example, an ANN with a [128, 128] architecture has an input layer with 45 inputs, two dense hidden layers with 128 neurons, and an output layer with five outputs. The rest of the model hyperparameters for the ANN and XGB models (e.g., batch size for ANNs and leaf samples for XGB) were selected via Bayesian search and optimization. Our dataset consisted of one million stages, with 90% allocated for training and 10% for testing. For each stage in the dataset, we identified the method with the shortest runtime that met the specified threshold E or, if not found, we selected the most accurate one among all five methods. Before training, we normalized all input features using the standard scaler [33] to ensure that no input dominates over the others. Finally, model generation was performed using the XGBoost [35], PyTorch [34], and Sklearn [33] frameworks,

Conversely, the ANN and XGB models are high-performance, have a low memory footprint, and can utilize both CPU and GPU acceleration. Considering these observations, we selected XGB models with 120 estimators for each ML model configuration, as they provide the best balance between accuracy (high F1 score) and performance (low inference/training time).

4. Experimental evaluation

Figs. 4(a)–4(c) illustrate the accuracy (F1 score), training and inference times, and memory requirements of the evaluated ML models. As can be seen, the XGB models outperform ANNs and KNNs, with the more complex (having 120 or more estimators) and those employing higher threshold criteria exhibiting the highest scores (F1 up to 0.934). Increasing the number of estimators enhances predictive performance while targeting a higher accuracy threshold favors selecting the optimal timing analysis method. This highlights the robustness of XGB models, particularly in modeling highly variable, non-continuous spaces using tabular input data. Moreover, Figs. 5(a)–5(b) further demonstrate the impact of our features, showcasing the substantial feature gain of our selection and the excellent classification performance of the XGB models. In comparison, KNNs and ANNs exhibit similar accuracy trends, with accuracy only improving as the threshold E increases, achieving F1 up to 0.853 and 0.881, respectively. As shown in Figs. 4(b)–4(c), the deterministic nature of the evaluated KNN algorithms ensures consistent prediction outcomes, whereas increasing the number of neurons in the ANN hidden layers leads to minor accuracy variations (e.g., [128, 128] vs. [256, 256]). Notably, more complex ANN configurations sometimes performed worse at higher thresholds (i.e., E< 8%) due to overfitting issues and the significant effort required for hyperparameter tuning.

The framework allows for parallel computation of stage timing using CPU acceleration through multi-threading [43], and the ML models can leverage either CPU or GPU acceleration during inference by setting a single configuration parameter [35]. Specifically, the ML-based methods were evaluated under both acceleration scenarios to assess their performance, while M1-M5 exploit only CPU acceleration. Our experiments were conducted on one million representative stages that cover various driver/receiver combinations and interconnect topologies. The driver and receiver gates are selected from the ASU ASAP 7 nm, Silvaco FreePDK 15 nm, and Silvaco FreePDK 45 nm Process Design Kits (PDKs) [44,45]. The interconnects vary in length and are routed across multiple metal layers, with up to 16 layers available depending on the PDK. Consequently, the RC values of the interconnect models span a wide range, determined by the resistance and capacitance of the selected metal layers and the resistance of the connecting vias. Moreover, the stages are generated so that driver input slew (Sin drv), driver output effective capacitance (Ceff), and receiver output capacitance (Cout rcv ) cover the entire range of the precharacterized LUTs and even beyond that. The golden stage delay and slew values were obtained using the Xyce Parallel Electronic Simulator [10]. Finally, all experiments were run on a Linux workstation, equipped with a 3.70 GHz 12-thread Ryzen 5600X CPU, an NVIDIA RTX 3700 GPU, and 16 GB of RAM.

Considering the runtime results, we can observe a clear correlation between the complexity of each base method and its respective performance. In particular, M1 exhibits the highest runtime (12.5 s), whereas the simplest M3 method records the lowest (0.55 s). Moreover, our proposed ML approaches, ML1 and ML2, achieve substantial speedups compared to the most accurate method (M1) while maintaining high accuracy levels. With CPU-accelerated inference, ML1 and ML2 expedite timing analysis by 2.29× and 3.28×, respectively. When leveraging GPU acceleration, the speedups increase to 2.62× and 3.86×, respectively, representing a 15% improvement over the CPU-accelerated implementation. Furthermore, the overall overhead introduced by the ML model to timing analysis is reduced to 3%–4%, compared to 8%–10% corresponding to CPU-accelerated execution. Therefore, when GPU hardware is available, the resulting performance gains can expedite timing analysis without compromising predictive accuracy or ease of use. It is crucial to note that adopting more resource-intensive signoffaccurate methods, such as AWE and RICE [13,14], as output classes of our ML models would further amplify the performance and improve the accuracy of the proposed approach. Since computationally expensive methods demand longer runtimes, selecting more efficient alternatives yields greater relative speedups, as model inference represents a constant yet smaller cost compared to the total stage timing analysis time. Thus, this further enhances the practicality and effectiveness of our ML models, particularly in performance-critical applications like timing-driven iterative optimization flows [1].

5. Conclusions

Table 3 presents the mean stage delay and slew RE against SPICE, the runtime, and the speedup over the most accurate method (M1) achieved by each approach. Firstly, it is evident that accuracy decreases as we transition from the most complex multiple Ceff-based (M1) to the simplest Ctotal-based (M3) method. Secondly, moment-based methods (M3-M5) generally exhibit lower accuracy, with M4-M5 being rarely selected by the ML models, as depicted in the confusion matrices of Fig. 5. In contrast, our ML approaches achieve accuracy in line with their respective thresholds and our available methods. ML1 closely follows the best available method (M1), exhibiting stage delay and slew RE of 2.51% and 2.85%, respectively. Moreover, ML2 effectively maintains errors well below the target 4% threshold, achieving 3.39% and 3.87% stage delay and slew RE. Note that ML1 and ML2 achieve the same accuracy when utilizing CPU or GPU acceleration, as it does not affect accuracy, but only runtime performance.

In this work, we presented TimeBoost, a simple yet powerful learning-based approach to accelerate timing analysis. The proposed method employs XGBoost classifiers, which, given representative features of a gate-level stage, predict the most suitable timing analysis method, exploiting the available accuracy/runtime trade-offs. Experimental results demonstrate that our ML-driven approach delivers a 3.86× speedup over our best available stage timing analysis method while maintaining commendable accuracy.
