# Machine Learning and GPU-Accelerated Static Timing Analysis for Intent-Robust Timing Closure

## Abstract

Static timing analysis (STA) remains the dominant formalism for timing verification and sign-off, but modern RTL-to-GDSII flows increasingly suffer from a mismatch between (i) the timing models and metrics used early in the flow and (ii) the sign-off reality after routing, extraction, and final convergence. This mismatch forces expensive iterations in placement, routing, and engineering change order (ECO) cycles, and it is amplified by scaling-driven effects (greater interconnect dominance, stronger process-voltage-temperature variability, coupling/crosstalk, aging, and power integrity excursions). Recent research addresses this bottleneck with two complementary thrusts: machine learning (ML) for timing and timing-adjacent prediction/optimization across design stages, and GPU/heterogeneous acceleration to reduce the runtime of “true” timing engines (including multicorner STA and path-based analysis). This review synthesizes fifty representative works spanning early timing prediction, optimization-aware learning, routing-stage correlation and sign-off alignment, multicorner learning with reliability safeguards, aging/noise-aware modeling, algorithmic advances in statistical timing and path sensitization, coupling-aware STA, and GPU-accelerated STA/PBA engines. We propose a unifying “intent-robust timing closure” framing, where the objective is not only low prediction error at one fixed stage, but stable usefulness under design intent changes (optimization, ECO, corner explosion, technology migration, aging/noise regimes) while preserving sign-off trustworthiness. We distill emerging design patterns that improve practical deployability: (a) learning *decisions* (method selection, dominant corner choice) rather than replacing physics or sign-off; (b) multimodal representations (netlist + layout + routing topology surrogates); (c) transfer learning across timing arcs, designs, and technology nodes; (d) uncertainty-aware or “safe fallback” strategies; and (e) GPU-friendly reformulations of STA kernels and path enumeration subroutines.

Keywords: static timing analysis, timing closure, machine learning, graph neural networks, multicorner analysis, GPU acceleration, path-based analysis, statistical timing, aging, power supply noise, routing topology optimization

## Introduction

Timing closure is an iterative optimization-and-verification loop, not a single analysis pass. Contemporary flows repeatedly invoke STA to check setup and hold constraints, guide sizing and buffering, prioritize routing, and validate ECO edits. That loop becomes expensive when multicorner analysis is in the critical path of physical design iterations [16]. It also becomes expensive when high-fidelity graph propagation and incremental timing updates dominate runtime inside sign-off-oriented engines [22]. Classical SSTA surveys explain why the problem scales badly once variability, correlation, and pessimism reduction must be treated systematically rather than by a small set of deterministic corners [49].

Two forces shape the current literature. The first is **stage mismatch**. A placement-stage or global-routing-stage predictor can look accurate locally yet still drive the flow in the wrong direction if it is poorly aligned with post-route sign-off. The GR-to-DR correction framework in [10] is motivated exactly by that mismatch. The sign-off-driven routing-topology work in [7] addresses the same issue from the optimization side. PRO-TIME in [8] makes the argument even more directly: a prerouting predictor trained without timing optimization in the loop is mis-specified for real deployment.

The second force is **runtime pressure inside trusted timing engines**. GPU acceleration has become important not because STA is conceptually broken, but because its more trustworthy variants are expensive. Path-based analysis remains valuable for pessimism reduction but is computationally heavy [21]. Multicorner graph propagation also becomes a throughput problem at modern scenario counts [22].

Recent review literature already surveys machine-learning-based STA in agile design across the RTL-to-GDSII flow [20]. Broader timing surveys remain essential for statistical analysis fundamentals [49]. Reliability-oriented reviews expand the timing discussion beyond process variation to aging and lifetime effects [39]. What is still missing is a synthesis that treats ML methods, statistical timing methods, reliability-aware modeling, and GPU acceleration as parts of the same closure problem.

This review uses the term *intent-robust timing closure* as that synthesis. A timing-closure method is intent-robust if it remains useful when the design intent shifts during the flow. Optimization-induced netlist restructuring is one such shift [8]. ECO-driven topology change is another [19]. Technology-node migration introduces a further shift in both data distribution and physical behavior [6]. Aging and runtime degradation reshape timing priorities yet again [15]. Dynamic power-supply noise perturbs delay under operating conditions that are not captured by nominal STA assumptions [12]. Within this framing, ML and GPU acceleration are complementary rather than competing ideas: ML helps determine what to predict, correct, or optimize, while acceleration helps deliver trustworthy timing feedback fast enough to stay inside the optimization loop.

## Static timing analysis and robustness foundations

Classical STA propagates arrival times and required times through a timing graph and reports slack-based metrics such as worst negative slack (WNS) and total negative slack (TNS). However, even the semantics of slack require care. Vygen shows that in the standard STA model, a positive slack value does not automatically imply tolerance to an equal extra delay; the paper therefore redefines slack to recover the intended optimization meaning and continuity properties [50]. That point matters for modern ML papers because several of them learn or optimize slack-derived targets rather than raw path delay. Endpoint slack prediction is central in [5]. WNS and TNS prediction are central in [29]. Pre-route optimization feedback also relies on those metrics in [17]. Path-delay variation learning in [33] is related but not identical, which shows that the exact choice of label is already a modeling decision.

Robustness challenges arise because STA is intentionally simplified. It abstracts waveform behavior, ignores many sensitization effects, and often substitutes corner values for full uncertainty distributions. SSTA was developed to reduce that pessimism by treating delays as random variables from the start [49]. A recent optimization-lens treatment of histogram-based SSTA shows that even classical timing analysis can be reframed as binary-integer or geometric programming, not only as a propagation heuristic [18]. Delay-correlation-aware path-based SSTA pushes the analysis further by propagating conditional moments and estimating both cell-to-cell and path-to-path delay correlations [45]. Smart Monte Carlo methods provide another bridge between brute-force simulation and analytic SSTA by reducing sample cost while preserving statistical fidelity [47].

A recurring hardness dimension is *path trueness*. The graph-critical path under STA may be false or rarely sensitized in actual operation. Extended STA addresses this explicitly by quantifying sensitization probabilities and bounding timing-error probability over input combinations [37]. Viability-analysis-based critical-path identification also shows that the most important path under variation is not always the nominal graph-longest path, while still offering major speedups over exhaustive search [40]. Even the definition of gate importance changes under statistical optimization: adjacency criticality in [35] modifies conventional gate criticality by accounting for the fan-out cone, which is more aligned with timing-yield improvement than a purely local notion of criticality.

Other physical effects add further layers of robustness requirements. Coupling-aware STA must solve an iterative dependence problem rather than a single-pass graph problem; FA-STAC therefore combines coupling models with convergence-acceleration techniques and reports substantial runtime gains over traditional iterative approaches [48]. Time-varying SSTA for aging shows that path criticality itself evolves over time and can be captured efficiently through breakpoint reasoning [38]. BTI-aware temporal statistical timing analysis makes the same point at the device-physics level by combining aging variability with path correlation effects [44].

Robustness also depends on how primitive timing quantities are modeled. Interdependent setup and hold constraints can reduce delay uncertainty and improve synchronous robustness more directly than independent-constraint abstractions [46]. That same idea reappears in more recent work on interdependent flip-flop timing models for NVT circuits, where timing analysis and optimization are improved by modeling the constraint interaction explicitly rather than treating setup, hold, and clock-to-Q delay as separate tables [31]. At the gate-model level, current-fluctuation-aware delay modeling allows conventional STA frameworks to remain usable over wide PVT ranges by translating variation effects into modified load and waveform quantities [42]. Analytical delay modeling in the near-subthreshold regime serves a similar purpose when voltage scaling makes process randomness especially important [36]. Stochastic logical effort adds yet another yield-oriented abstraction for variation-aware timing estimation [41]. These modeling papers matter for ML as well, because the quality of learned predictions is limited by the physical quality of the labels and features used to train them.

## Machine-learning timing models across the RTL-to-GDSII stack

A central observation across the ML literature is that timing-related prediction tasks differ by *abstraction level* (RTL, preplacement, placement/pre-route, routing, sign-off) and by *target granularity* (cell/arc delay, net parasitics, path delay, endpoint arrival/slack, global WNS/TNS). The same model class may behave very differently across these regimes. This section synthesizes ML approaches by where they can be integrated in the flow and by how they interact with the true timing engine.

### RTL and preplacement prediction

At RTL, wrong architectural assumptions can cascade into expensive downstream iterations. The neural-network framework in [27] predicts pin-to-pin delay for RTL combinational circuits, jointly estimates slew and delay, and reports an 8.4x speedup over running the full downstream flow during early exploration. The main lesson is practical rather than ideological: RTL prediction is most useful when it ranks risky regions and filters bad design choices early, not when it claims to replace sign-off.

Preplacement estimation tackles an even harder setting because net lengths are still unknown. The customized GNN framework in [29] first predicts net lengths before placement and then uses those estimates to build a preplacement timing estimator. Because the model inserts an explicit net-length surrogate between topology and timing, it reports stronger correlation with post-placement timing than approaches that rely only on coarse wirelength heuristics [29].

### Placement-stage guidance and optimization-aware learning

Placement is a leverage point because small geometric changes can reshape both routability and timing. The GNN-based placement-guidance framework in [1] predicts candidate sizing and buffering actions and identifies path groups likely to violate timing, reporting sizable WNS, TNS, and violating-path improvements on OpenCores benchmarks. The important point is not just the prediction accuracy: the model is trained to propose actionable placement-stage interventions [1].

A related but distinct line of work asks whether the timing model itself can participate in optimization. The gradient-boosting approach in [3] converts its predictor into a derivable function and injects that function into iterative placement. That move is crucial for deployment because it changes ML from a reporting tool into an optimization signal. The complex-network timing model in [2] points in the same direction from another angle: by augmenting standard circuit features with complex-network descriptors, it improves prediction quality for slack, delay, WNS, and TNS at the placement stage. Together, [2] and [3] suggest that richer representations matter, but differentiability or optimization compatibility matters just as much.

Another conservative deployment pattern is post-placement correction rather than outright replacement. The Random-Forest case study in [25] improves cell-delay accuracy against a stronger reference estimator and sharply reduces RMSE. The ensemble-tree extension in [13] continues that line and reports improved average accuracy on both seen and unseen designs. These studies are important because they cast ML as a correlation-restoration layer between abstractions, which is usually easier to trust in industrial timing flows than a full learned replacement.

### Pre-routing and routing-stage prediction and sign-off alignment

A large fraction of recent work targets *prerouting timing prediction* because routing is expensive and early prediction errors multiply across place-and-route iterations. The two-level GNN+CNN framework in [17] first predicts net resistance, capacitance, and arc length, then predicts delay and slew, and finally uses those quantities in a timing-optimization loop. The graph-attention framework in [5] instead emphasizes propagation structure, alternating net and cell updates in a way inspired by timing engines themselves. Despite different architectures, both papers are strongest when they imitate timing propagation structure rather than treating timing as an unstructured regression problem.

A major barrier to prerouting deployment is that the flow keeps changing the design while the predictor is trying to model it. PRO-TIME addresses this directly by training on optimization-aware targets and by treating netlist restructuring as a feature-mismatch problem rather than a nuisance variable [8]. Its multimodal representation combines endpoint-wise netlist embeddings with layout information, making the predictor more robust to the same optimization loop it is supposed to assist [8].

Technology migration creates another distribution shift. The transfer-learning framework in [6] disentangles and aligns path features across nodes, then reweights source-node data according to cell-type distribution. That is a more realistic transfer strategy than assuming that a model trained at one node can simply be fine-tuned blindly at another [6].

At the routing boundary, the gap between global-route estimates and detailed-route truth is especially important. The GR-to-DR correction framework in [10] learns that gap explicitly, adds macro-blockage features for difficult designs, and shows that using the corrected estimates during post-GR optimization improves post-DR slack without worsening congestion. This is one of the clearest examples in the corpus where the success criterion is not predictor error in isolation but whether early decisions improve later sign-off behavior [10].

A complementary line of work optimizes routing topology itself against sign-off outcomes. The concurrent routing-topology method in [7] uses a learned evaluator to guide Steiner-point adjustment, topology reconstruction, and simplification, reporting measurable sign-off WNS and TNS gains together with shorter optimization runtime. The shift here is conceptual: ML is no longer only predicting sign-off behavior; it is actively reshaping a routing object that the sign-off flow will later validate [7].

### ECO acceleration, multicorner prediction, and “safe” ML integration

ECO timing is a prototypical intent shift because the design changes materially while turnaround time expectations stay short. TSTL-GNN models this setting with a two-stage transfer-learning structure in which transition prediction feeds delay prediction [19]. That design choice is important because it follows the causal structure of timing calculation instead of treating ECO delay as an isolated target. The paper also reports that the method remains effective on modified paths after substantial post-ECO structural change, which is exactly the kind of robustness ordinary static regressors often lack [19].

Multicorner timing is another axis where raw regression is not enough. The dominant-corner strategy in [28] tackles the combinatorial question of which corners to run and which to predict, reporting high prediction accuracy and more than 2x timing-closure acceleration in the industrial case study. The multicorner acceleration framework in [16] goes further by building a reliability layer on top of the predictor. Its Bayesian decision strategy selectively triggers extra STA runs when confidence is insufficient. This is one of the strongest examples in the corpus of a *safe ML integration* pattern: prediction is allowed to save runtime, but only under an explicit fallback policy [16].

### ML embedded into timing libraries and noise/aging-aware modeling

Some of the most impactful timing errors arise not from graph propagation but from inaccurate primitives: cell characterization, interpolation, and modeling under additional physical conditions.

For library characterization, transfer learning across timing arcs is used in [4] to reduce SPICE characterization burden while preserving delay accuracy. Deep-learning cell-delay modeling in [24] targets the same primitive layer from a different angle by replacing or augmenting conventional LUT-style timing abstractions with learned waveform-aware models. The interpolation paper in [11] attacks another important primitive: repeated table lookups. By learning interpolation over timing tables and parallelizing the procedure on GPU, it improves table accuracy without asking the full timing engine to change its outer structure.

For dynamic operating conditions, the PSN-aware timing engine in [12] integrates MLP-based arc predictors into STA through just-in-time compilation, which is a good example of ML embedded *inside* an engine rather than bolted on outside it. Aging-aware primitive modeling follows the same logic. The heterogeneous-graph cell-timing model in [14] captures device-level structure inside cells for aging-aware timing estimation. The path-level multi-view graph model in [15] then lifts aging prediction to full timing paths. Aging-aware critical-path selection in [23] focuses on ranking rather than absolute delay prediction, which is often the more actionable objective during closure. AVATAR widens the scope further by treating aging and variation within a dynamic timing-analyzer framework for error-efficient computing rather than only conventional sign-off [26].

ML has also been used inside more classical statistical timing settings. The neural-network timing algorithm in [30] models the mean and standard deviation of gate delay under PVT variation and combines that with viability analysis. The path-delay-variation framework in [33] learns variation directly for corner transfer. NN-SSTA goes one step deeper into SSTA mechanics by replacing expensive statistical max and convolution operations with learned approximations, reporting substantial average speedup relative to a traditional discrete SSTA flow [34]. These papers matter because they show ML acting not only as a predictor of timing outcomes, but also as an approximation layer for the operations that make statistical timing expensive.

## GPU-accelerated and heterogeneous timing analysis engines

ML can reduce the number of expensive sign-off invocations, but timing closure also benefits from making the trusted timing engines themselves faster. GPU acceleration is attractive because major STA kernels - levelization, per-arc delay computation, graph propagation, and corner-level data replication - expose substantial parallelism when mapped carefully [22]. PBA is especially attractive because its computational intensity is high even when its memory behavior is irregular [21].

The heterogeneous CPU-GPU STA engine in [22] accelerates levelization, delay computation, propagation, incremental updates, and multicorner analysis through GPU-oriented data structures and concurrent task partitioning. The reported speedups - up to 4.07x for single-corner analysis and up to 25.67x for multicorner analysis - matter because they target the exact analyses that designers are reluctant to call repeatedly when turnaround time is tight [22].

The GPU PBA framework in [21] attacks a different bottleneck. Rather than accelerating the whole STA loop, it focuses on the pessimism-reduction stage that many flows use sparingly because of cost. The paper reports a 543x speedup over the stated baseline PBA algorithm on a 1.6 million-gate design, plus additional gains from further optimization. The larger implication is that higher-fidelity path analysis can move earlier into the closure loop once runtime stops being prohibitive [21].

GPU acceleration also appears inside hybrid ML-assisted components. TimeBoost uses GPU-accelerated XGBoost, but importantly it learns *method selection* rather than direct timing prediction [9]. The interpolation work in [11] also uses GPU parallelism, not to accelerate the outer graph engine, but to keep a learned inner-loop primitive practical. In both cases, GPU is best understood not as brute-force compute for its own sake, but as the hardware layer that makes deployable hybrid timing systems feasible.

## Intent-robust timing closure workflows combining ML and acceleration

The fifty papers collectively suggest that timing closure becomes intent-robust only when three kinds of robustness are addressed together: robustness to flow-stage abstraction, robustness to design transformation, and robustness to operating and lifetime uncertainty. The literature converges on a small set of workflow patterns.

### Robustness to stage abstraction and toolflow mismatch

Early-stage models routinely suffer from missing routing detail and approximated parasitics. The correction strategy in [10] shows one viable response: learn the gap between early estimates and later truth. The routing-topology work in [7] shows another: optimize the early object directly against sign-off-sensitive feedback. PRO-TIME in [8] makes the broader methodological point that predictors must be trained under the same flow semantics they will face in deployment. Together, these works argue that stage mismatch is not a minor accuracy issue; it is a training-target issue.

### Robustness to design transformations, including ECO and optimization

Design transformations such as resizing, buffering, and ECO rewiring are unavoidable during closure. Placement guidance in [1] predicts actions rather than only metrics. The derivable timing model in [3] is explicitly designed to survive integration into optimization. TSTL-GNN in [19] models a post-change setting instead of pretending the distribution is static. The shared lesson is that ML must either model the transformation, tolerate it, or sit behind an explicit trust-and-fallback mechanism.

### Robustness to PVT/corner explosion and to aging/noise regimes

Multicorner prediction in [28] is useful because it reduces the number of expensive corners, but the safer system in [16] is more instructive because it adds an explicit reliability layer. Cross-node transfer in [6] addresses data scarcity when technology shifts. Aging-aware timing prediction in [15] and critical-path ranking in [23] show that lifetime effects alter not only delays but also prioritization. Dynamic PSN-aware timing in [12] extends the same argument to runtime electrical conditions. Robust timing closure is therefore not a single-axis generalization problem; it is a collection of interacting shifts that must each be modeled or bounded.

### A practical synthesis: learn the decision, accelerate the truth

The most deployable systems in the corpus avoid the simplistic goal of replacing STA outright.

1. **Learn the decision layer.** TimeBoost selects among established analysis methods instead of predicting timing directly [9]. The multicorner framework in [16] decides when prediction is safe and when more STA is necessary. The dominant-corner approach in [28] decides which corners deserve actual analysis.

2. **Learn the correlation bridge.** The GR-to-DR model in [10] aligns earlier optimization with later truth. The routing-topology framework in [7] uses a learned sign-off evaluator to guide earlier routing choices. PRO-TIME in [8] aligns the training target with optimization-aware prerouting deployment.

3. **Accelerate the trusted engine.** The GPU STA engine in [22] speeds up the graph-level analyses that designers already trust. The GPU PBA engine in [21] makes pessimism reduction practical at larger scale. The interpolation work in [11] keeps a learned inner-loop primitive fast enough to live inside a real engine.

Under this synthesis, intent robustness becomes an engineering property rather than a slogan: preserve a sign-off anchor, close stage-correlation gaps, and make high-fidelity feedback frequent enough to influence optimization.

## Open challenges and research directions

Across the reviewed studies, several research gaps remain.

Data realism and confidentiality are still major bottlenecks. Most ML models depend on expensive labels from proprietary tool flows. Transfer across arcs helps [4]. Transfer across ECO states helps [19]. Transfer across technology nodes helps [6]. But the field still lacks a clean theory for separating design-specific timing knowledge from node-specific or tool-specific timing knowledge.

Correctness guarantees remain weaker than they should be. The Bayesian decision layer in [16] is a strong step toward safe deployment, but broader calibration and out-of-distribution detection remain underdeveloped. This is especially important because even the meaning of the target metric can be unstable if slack semantics are mishandled [50]. A promising direction is to combine learned predictors with sensitization-aware notions of criticality drawn from [37] and [40].

Graph-model scalability is still unresolved. The graph-attention approach in [5] recognizes the large-graph problem directly. Hierarchical timing-model extraction in [43] suggests one route for reducing problem size without throwing away correlation structure. GPU acceleration in [21] and [22] suggests another route, but irregular graph workloads and incremental updates still make naive scaling difficult.

Integration into optimization also remains immature. The derivable timing model in [3] and the sign-off-driven routing work in [7] show that closed-loop integration is possible. Yet most papers still report standalone predictor accuracy rather than final sign-off QoR under a fixed runtime budget. That should change.

Finally, robustness is multidimensional. Copula-based gate-delay modeling in [32] addresses dependency structure. Stochastic logical effort in [41] addresses yield-oriented variation modeling. Smart Monte Carlo in [47] preserves simulation grounding while reducing cost. Coupling-aware STA in [48] handles interconnect interaction directly. The surveys in [39] and [49] remain useful reminders that timing uncertainty is broader than the current ML wave. The most promising future systems will likely be hybrid: ML decides what deserves attention next, statistically grounded timing engines decide what is actually safe to conclude, and acceleration keeps the loop practical.
